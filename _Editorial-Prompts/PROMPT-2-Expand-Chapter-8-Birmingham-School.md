### Priority 2: The Distributional Analysis (Currently Lines 95-146)

**Current state:** Compressed into "TEXT BOX: WHO WON AND WHO LOST" 

**Enhanced approach (add ~500 words):**

Integrate this into flowing narrative that shows the mechanism:

> The pattern was systematic and calculable. When gold convertibility was restored in 1821, the deflation that followed transferred approximately **£160 million** in real wealth from productive workers to bondholders—roughly £10-12 billion in modern terms.
>
> The mechanism was simple arithmetic made catastrophic by scale. A creditor who lent £1,000 at 5% interest in 1820 expected to receive £1,050 in 1821. Under the gold standard's deflationary pressure—prices fell approximately 20% by 1824—that £1,050 bought **25% more goods** than the original £1,000 had bought in 1820. The creditor received not just the contracted 5% interest but an additional 20% real return through deflation.
>
> For debtors, the arithmetic ran in reverse. A farmer who borrowed £1,000 in 1820 still owed £1,050 in 1821. But wheat prices had fallen from 108 shillings per quarter to 45 shillings—a 58% drop. To generate £1,050 in revenue, the farmer now had to produce and sell **more than twice as much wheat** as when he took the loan. His real debt burden had effectively doubled while his income was cut in half.
>
> This wasn't a side effect; it was the core feature of gold standard deflation. The establishment explicitly justified the system by claiming it protected creditors' "legitimate expectations" against inflation. What they didn't say—perhaps didn't even recognize—was that deflation created **illegitimate windfalls** for creditors at the expense of productive workers and entrepreneurs.
>
> Government debt stood at approximately £800 million in 1820—roughly 200% of GDP. The 20% deflation increased the real value of this debt by £160 million. This wasn't wealth created; it was wealth redistributed. Every pound of the bondholders' deflation windfall came from farmers producing more grain for the same nominal payment, manufacturers cutting wages or shedding workers, and families losing land they'd worked for generations.
>
> Attwood understood this mechanism with crystalline clarity. His Birmingham workers weren't suffering because they'd become lazy or unskilled. They were suffering because monetary policy had systematically redistributed purchasing power from wage earners to wealth holders. The orthodoxy framed this as "sound money" and "protecting legitimate expectations." Attwood recognized it as class warfare conducted through monetary arithmetic.

### Priority 3: The "Monomania" Dismissal (Currently ~200 words)

**Current approach:** Briefly mentions Disraeli's quote, notes dismissal as "currency cranks"

**Enhanced approach (add ~300 words):**

Make this personal and devastating:

> The establishment's response wasn't to engage Attwood's arguments but to pathologize him. Benjamin Disraeli, the future Prime Minister, described him as "a provincial banker labouring under **financial monomania**"—an obsession with money that supposedly blinded him to economic reality.
>
> Imagine the experience from Attwood's perspective. You've built a successful business over decades. You've watched skilled workers—men whose families you know, whose children play with yours—lose their livelihoods through no fault of their own. You've identified the precise mechanism causing this suffering: monetary contraction imposed by policy choice. You've articulated solutions that would work. You've organized mass demonstrations showing that 40,000 workers support reform. You've gathered 1,280,958 signatures demonstrating mass support.
>
> And Parliament's response is to declare you mentally unsound. Not wrong, not mistaken—*obsessed*. Psychologically damaged. Unfit for serious consideration.
>
> This wasn't mere academic disagreement—it was character assassination masquerading as diagnosis. Economic journals ignored Birmingham School publications. Universities didn't teach their theories. When establishment economists mentioned Attwood at all, it was to ridicule him as an example of what happens when businessmen dabble in economic theory beyond their competence.
>
> His proto-Keynesian insights about the multiplier effect, his understanding that unemployment represented wasted capacity rather than natural equilibrium, his recognition that paper money could function without gold backing—all were buried under dismissive labels that questioned his sanity rather than his arguments.
>
> The personal cost was real. Attwood had sacrificed time, energy, and social capital organizing the Birmingham Political Union and the Chartist movement. He'd made powerful enemies among the bondholding class. He'd been ridiculed in Parliament and dismissed in print. And for what? To watch Parliament reject 1,280,958 signatures with barely a second thought. To see his economic insights erased from the historical record. To be remembered—if at all—as a crank who didn't understand "real" economics.
>
> By the time Attwood died in 1856, the forgetting was complete.
