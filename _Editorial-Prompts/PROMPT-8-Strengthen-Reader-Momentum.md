```
### Money Creation in Practice: A Merchant's Experience

To see how this worked in practice, consider Thomas Fletcher, a London cloth merchant seeking to expand his business in 1703.

Fletcher needed £500 to purchase raw wool from Yorkshire and hire additional weavers. Traditional moneylenders charged 15-20% interest—rates that would consume any profit margin. But the Bank of England, holding the government charter and Parliament's backing, could lend at 6%.

Fletcher presented his books to the Bank, demonstrating regular sales and creditworthiness. The Bank's clerk examined his accounts, verified his existing property as collateral, and approved the loan. What happened next would have seemed miraculous to medieval merchants:

**The Bank created £500 by writing it into their ledger.**

On the Bank's books:
- ASSETS side: "Loan to Thomas Fletcher, £500" (Fletcher owes the Bank)
- LIABILITIES side: "Bank notes issued, £500" (Bank owes Fletcher these notes)

Fletcher received £500 in Bank of England notes—paper slips promising to pay the bearer on demand. These notes were not backed by £500 in gold sitting in the vault. The Bank held perhaps £100-150 in reserves against this £500 in notes. The rest was credit—created by the lending process itself.

Fletcher used these notes to:
1. Pay Yorkshire wool suppliers (notes circulated to Yorkshire)
2. Hire weavers (notes circulated to working-class London)
3. Purchase equipment (notes circulated to manufacturers)

Each recipient accepted the notes as money because they knew:
- They could discharge tax obligations with them
- The Bank of England would redeem them for gold if needed (though most never bothered)
- Other merchants would accept them in turn

Fletcher repaid the £500 plus £30 interest over two years. The £500 in notes remained in circulation, now backed by new loans to other merchants. The £30 interest compensated the Bank for default risk and covered operating costs.

**This was money creation through credit extension**—the same process modern banks use today when they make loans by typing numbers into accounts. No gold mine needed, no government printing press, just double-entry bookkeeping backed by institutional credibility.

```

- Specific numbers (£500, 6% interest, £30) make it concrete
- Readers understand what notes did (circulated to Yorkshire, weavers, manufacturers)
- Modern parallel noted ("same process modern banks use") creates relevance

```
### Inside the Conference: Power Disguised as Negotiation

The conference opened July 1, 1944, with 730 delegates from 44 nations gathering in the Mount Washington Hotel's grand ballroom. The setting was carefully chosen—isolated location in New Hampshire's White Mountains prevented delegates from consulting home governments before committing to positions. Three weeks of intensive negotiation lay ahead.

Or so it appeared.

In reality, the core decisions had been made months earlier in Washington. What looked like an international conference between sovereign equals was actually a carefully choreographed demonstration of American power wrapped in the language of multilateral cooperation.

**The First Day: Establishing Who Controls the Agenda**

Secretary of the Treasury Henry Morgenthau opened the conference with welcoming remarks emphasizing "cooperation" and "mutual benefit." But the real message came when American officials distributed the working documents: pre-drafted proposals for the International Monetary Fund, already reflecting American positions on voting weights, governance structure, and reserve currency status.

British delegate Sir George Bolton later recalled his shock: "We arrived expecting to negotiate. Instead we found ourselves presented with faits accomplis dressed up as 'discussion drafts.'"

**The Keynes-White Dynamic: Celebrity vs. Control**

Keynes arrived as something of an economic rock star—his 1919 *Economic Consequences of the Peace* had predicted Versailles Treaty disaster with devastating accuracy. His 1936 *General Theory* had revolutionized macroeconomics. When he spoke, delegates listened.

But Harry Dexter White held the leverage that mattered: American gold reserves (80% of global total) and the implicit threat that America could simply walk away. While Keynes commanded intellectual attention, White controlled the procedural machinery—committee assignments, working group agendas, document drafting schedules.

The power dynamic became visible in small moments:

When Keynes protested an American draft in October 1943—"This is intolerable. It is yet another Talmud. We had better simply break off negotiations"—White's reply dripped with condescension: "We will try to produce something which Your Highness can understand."

When Keynes proposed significant changes to voting weights, American delegates simply scheduled discussions for late-night sessions when British participants were exhausted, then called for votes before opposition could organize.

**The Bancor's Quiet Death**

Keynes's International Clearing Union proposal—the bancor system with symmetric adjustment—never received a formal floor vote. Instead, it died through procedural suffocation:

- American drafts consistently used "dollar" as placeholder for reserve currency
- Working groups were structured to discuss IMF (White's plan) not ICU (Keynes's plan)  
- Time pressure (three-week deadline) prevented full consideration of alternatives
- Smaller nations, eager for Marshall Plan aid, hesitated to antagonize American hosts

By mid-conference, Keynes recognized the battle was lost. In a private letter to his wife, Lydia Lopokova: "I can influence the footnotes but not the text."

**July 13: The Decisive Moment**

The turning point came when India's A.D. Shroff asked what seemed like a simple clarifying question about "gold-convertible exchange":

> "I think it is high time that the USA delegation give us a definition of gold and gold convertible exchange."

Edward Bernstein of the U.S. delegation replied with careful vagueness:

> "Mr Chairman, it might be possible to give a definition...but it would involve a long discussion...it would be easier for this purpose to regard the United States dollar as what was intended when we speak of gold convertible exchange."

Dennis Robertson of the British delegation—notably, not Keynes—then proposed the text read: "payment of official gold and subscription should be expressed official holdings of gold and United States dollars."

The irony was profound: It was not Americans who placed the dollar at the system's center but a fissure within the British Empire (India seeking protection from sterling weakness) and a British economist acknowledging that only the dollar had sufficient strength.

**What "Compromise" Actually Meant**

American negotiators offered tactical concessions to create appearance of give-and-take:

- Fund size increased from initial proposals to $8.5 billion (Keynes had wanted $26 billion)
- "Scarce currency clause" allowed rationing currencies in short supply—theoretically applicable to dollars
- Exchange rates could adjust for "fundamental disequilibrium" (vague enough to mean anything)
- World Bank created for development finance (sweetener for debtor nations)

But on core issues—reserve currency, governance structure, symmetric vs. asymmetric adjustment—American positions were non-negotiable.

The final agreement was signed July 22, 1944. Newsreel footage shows delegates smiling, shaking hands, celebrating "international cooperation." What the cameras didn't capture: British delegates' exhaustion and resignation, smaller nations' recognition they'd had no real choice, and Keynes's private assessment that he'd salvaged what scraps he could from American dominance.

The kid who owned the ball was indeed captain of the team.
```
