- Build suspense: was fiat liberation or catastrophe?

## MAINTAINING BOOK'S ESTABLISHED QUALITIES

### Transition Style Consistency

**Study these exemplar transitions before writing:**

- **Chapter 3 → 4:** "Private money's failure would prove the necessity of what came next" (line ~138 of Ch 3)
- **Chapter 9 → 10:** "The breaking, when it came, would vindicate every warning the Banking School had issued" (end of Ch 9)
- **Chapter 10 → 11:** "As depression spread, policymakers gathered to ensure such chaos could never recur" (end of Ch 10)

**Pattern observed:**
1. Final section of chapter = 200-400 words
2. Opens with retrospective: "What we've just seen matters because..."
3. Pivots to forward preview: "The next phase would..."
4. Ends with anticipatory sentence creating momentum
5. **Never** chapter-ending cliffhangers (this isn't thriller fiction)—but clear forward motion

### Voice Consistency

**Key stylistic elements to maintain:**

1. **Measured tone:**
   - Not "This shocking revelation..." but "What makes this instructive is..."
   - Not "The dramatic collapse..." but "When the crisis came..."
   - Analytical observer, not breathless narrator

2. **Structural inevitability:**
   - Emphasize systems over individuals: "The mathematics were inexorable"
   - But then show human actors: "de Gaulle testing, Britain converting, speculators breaking"
   - Balance: forces + faces

3. **Contemporary relevance without forced parallels:**
   - Only mention modern analogies if genuinely illuminating
   - These transitions are historical—save contemporary framing for chapter openings

4. **Precise language:**
   - Specific dates when available: "August 15, 1971" not "summer 1971"
   - Exact statistics: "4.9% annual growth" not "nearly 5%"
   - This precision creates authority

### Integration Requirements

**Before finalizing each transition:**

1. **Read aloud:** Does it flow naturally from chapter ending to next chapter opening?

2. **Check temporal logic:** Are dates/periods clearly stated so readers don't get lost?

3. **Verify no redundancy:** Does this transition repeat what the next chapter will say? (Should preview, not duplicate)

4. **Confirm forward momentum:** Does final sentence create anticipation without being cliffhanger?

5. **Match chapter lengths:** Part III chapters average 5,400-6,600 words. Adding 300-400 word transitions shouldn't make any chapter feel overlong.

## PART IV SPECIFIC CONSIDERATIONS

### Why Part IV Feels Rushed (Diagnosis)

**Part III covers:** 1797-1850 (53 years) across 4 chapters = ~13 years per chapter average

**Part IV covers:** 1914-2023 (109 years) across 4 chapters = ~27 years per chapter average

The material density is inherently higher in Part IV. These transitions help by creating explicit guideposts.

### The Payoff Architecture

Part IV's arc:
- **Ch 10:** Gold standard catastrophe (interwar)
- **Ch 11:** Alternative that could have worked (Bretton Woods design)
- **Ch 12:** Why alternative failed (Triffin dilemma)
- **Ch 13:** What actually works (fiat with credible institutions)

**Transitions should reinforce this progression:**
- Ch 11 transition: "Success contained failure"
- Ch 12 transition: "Failure created opportunity for definitive test"
- Ch 6 transition: (backward chronological) "Let's see early proof paper worked"

### Avoiding Common Pitfalls

**DON'T:**
- Turn transitions into mini-chapters (keep to 200-400 words max)
- Repeat detailed content from chapters just completed
- Spoil the next chapter's revelations
- Use transitions to insert new arguments (they're connective tissue, not thesis)

**DO:**
- State clearly "we've seen X, now we'll see Y"
- Create anticipation through questions: "Could fiat work?" (not "Fiat worked!")
- Maintain analytical tone throughout
- Use transitions to orient readers temporally and conceptually
