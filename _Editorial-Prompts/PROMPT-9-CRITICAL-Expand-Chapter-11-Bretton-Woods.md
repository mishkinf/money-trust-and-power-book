```markdown
### How Power Defeats Logic: The Procedural Death of Bancor

The International Clearing Union never received a formal floor vote. Instead, it died through what might be called procedural suffocation‚Äîa series of small decisions that cumulatively made alternatives impossible.

**The Working Group Structure:**
American officials organized the conference into three commissions:
- Commission I: International Monetary Fund (White's plan)
- Commission II: International Bank (reconstruction lending)  
- Commission III: Other measures

Keynes's International Clearing Union wasn't a commission‚Äîit was "other measures." Working groups discussed "the IMF" as though its creation was settled fact, debating details of White's proposal rather than choosing between alternatives. When British delegates raised bancor features, American chairs ruled them "out of scope" for working groups focused on IMF implementation.

**The Document Control:**
American staff prepared all official drafts. These drafts used "dollar" as the placeholder currency throughout. Phrases like "IMF reserve currencies" and "dollar-gold convertibility" appeared in working documents before any votes occurred. By controlling which texts circulated, American officials defined what seemed "natural" or "practical."

Keynes proposed amendments to give sterling and other currencies co-equal reserve status. These amendments were accepted as text modifications but never changed the fundamental architecture. The IMF Articles of Agreement acknowledged that countries could hold reserves in currencies other than dollars‚Äîbut only the dollar was convertible to gold at fixed parity, making it the de facto reserve currency regardless of diplomatic language.

**The July 13 Moment:**
On July 13, Indian delegate Sir Chintaman Deshmukh Shroff asked a direct question during Commission I proceedings: "Why are we discussing the International Monetary Fund rather than the International Clearing Union? Professor Keynes's proposal solves the adjustment problem more elegantly."

The room fell silent. Delegates knew the answer but couldn't say it aloud: because the United States opposed bancor, and the United States held the gold.

White responded smoothly: "The Fund and the Union share similar objectives. We've incorporated the Union's insights into the Fund structure." This was diplomatic fiction. The IMF created no supranational currency, imposed no symmetric adjustment requirements, and left the United States as sole decision-maker on reserve currency status. But smaller nations understood the message: American proposals would pass; alternatives would die quietly.

**The Time Constraint:**
The conference was scheduled for three weeks. Keynes's ICU proposal required time to explain‚Äîthe clearing union mechanism, symmetric adjustment, the bancor unit of account. White's dollar plan was simpler: continue using dollars, make them gold-convertible, create an institution to lend dollars to countries in deficit. Complexity favored the status quo.

When British delegates requested extended time to build coalition support for bancor modifications, American officials agreed to extend the conference by three days‚Äîbut only after Keynes's July 19 heart attack made his participation uncertain. The extension was humanitarian gesture, not negotiating concession.

By July 22, when delegates signed the Final Act, the bancor existed only in the conference proceedings as "alternative proposal considered by Commission I."
```

**Why this works:**
- **Specific procedural details** (commission structure, document control, Shroff's question)
- **Shows mechanism of power** (not conspiracy, just leverage)
- **Creates tension** (July 13 silence, time constraints)
- **Explains outcome** (why bancor lost despite being technically superior)
