On May 7, 1832, Thomas Attwood stood before the House of Commons making an argument that would get him dismissed as a crank. Britain's economic distress, he insisted, stemmed not from moral failing or foreign competition but from **monetary contraction**. He had watched it happen in Birmingham.

Attwood was no wild-eyed radical. His workers were skilled craftsmen, his business well-established, his social position secure. ### The Victory of Orthodoxy—And Its Persistence

By 1850, the transformation was complete. The understanding that had been explicit in 1694—that the Bank of England created money "out of nothing" backed by parliamentary taxation—had become unthinkable in respectable economics.

**What was forgotten:**
- Adam Smith's 1776 insight: Princes can give value to paper money through taxation
- Bank Restriction 1797-1821: Paper money worked for 24 years without gold backing
- William Paterson's 1694 candor: The Bank profits from creating money "out of nothing"
- Taxation's role: Creating baseline currency demand independent of convertibility

**What replaced it:**
- Ricardo's Quantity Theory: Money supply must be constrained by gold
- Say's Law: Government spending can't address unemployment (general gluts impossible)
- Bank Charter Act 1844: Gold backing made automatic through law
- Household budget metaphor: Government must "find money" before spending

**The century ahead (1850-1950):**
Gold standard orthodoxy went unchallenged for a hundred years:
- 1873-1896: Great deflation benefited creditors, destroyed debtors
- 1925: Churchill returned to gold at overvalued parity, triggering General Strike
- 1929-1933: Fed adhered to Real Bills Doctrine, deepening Great Depression
- Only Keynes in the 1930s rediscovered what Thornton knew in 1802

**Why this matters today:**

The household budget metaphor—"governments must live within their means," "we can't afford Medicare for All," "national debt is out of control"—descends directly from this manufactured orthodoxy. The rhetoric claims economic necessity. The reality is political choice serving specific interests.

When politicians say "we can't afford it," what they mean is:
- We choose not to mobilize productive capacity for this purpose
- We prioritize creditor interests over full employment
- We prefer fiscal constraints over democratic choice about resource allocation

The forgetting of 1820-1850 enabled a century of unnecessary suffering. The lesson: Economic orthodoxy that serves narrow interests will claim to serve universal principles. Questioning it requires understanding the history of how it was manufactured.

The gold standard is gone. But the household budget metaphor it spawned persists—800 years after tally sticks proved taxation creates currency demand, and 327 years after the Bank of England created money "out of nothing."
