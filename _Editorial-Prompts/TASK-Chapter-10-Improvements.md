### Tale of Two Systems: Bancor vs. Dollar Standard

The Bretton Woods conference presented a choice between two fundamentally different monetary architectures. Understanding what was rejected illuminates why what was accepted eventually failed.

**KEYNES'S INTERNATIONAL CLEARING UNION (Bancor System)**

**Currency:**
- Supranational bancor (from "banque or"—bank gold)
- Defined in gold but NOT exchangeable for gold
- No single nation's currency privileged

**Liquidity Provision:**
- Unlimited overdraft facilities based on trade quotas
- Quotas proportionate to trade volume
- Automatic expansion when world trade grows
- No country faces currency shortage

**Adjustment Mechanism:**
- **SYMMETRIC**: Pressure on both surplus and deficit countries
- Surplus countries charged 5-10% interest on excess credits
- Persistent surpluses trigger mandatory currency appreciation
- "Use it or lose it" mechanism prevents hoarding

**Governance:**
- International Clearing Union with supranational authority
- Decisions made by international board
- No single country veto power

**The Triffin Dilemma:**
- **Solved**: No single currency needed for reserves
- Bancor created as needed for trade settlement
- No country must run deficits to supply liquidity

---

**WHITE'S STABILIZATION FUND (Dollar Standard)**

**Currency:**
- U.S. dollar as primary reserve currency
- Dollar convertible to gold at $35/ounce
- FOR OFFICIAL HOLDERS ONLY (not private citizens)
- Dollar privileged as "good as gold"

**Liquidity Provision:**
- Limited IMF resources ($8.5 billion initial quota)
- Finite pool, not automatic expansion
- Countries can exhaust IMF borrowing capacity
- Dollar shortages possible and inevitable

**Adjustment Mechanism:**
- **ASYMMETRIC**: Pressure only on deficit countries
- Surplus countries face no penalties
- No requirement to appreciate currency
- "Beggar thy neighbor" possible for surplus nations

**Governance:**
- IMF voting weighted by quota contributions
- U.S. contributes most, therefore veto power
- American interests effectively control decisions

**The Triffin Dilemma:**
- **Created**: World needs dollars for reserves
- U.S. must run deficits to supply dollars
- Persistent deficits undermine gold convertibility
- **STRUCTURAL CONTRADICTION**

### The Triffin Dilemma: The Impossible Math

Robert Triffin's 1959-60 testimony to Congress identified a structural contradiction that made Bretton Woods' collapse inevitable. The dilemma wasn't a policy mistake that could be corrected—it was **mathematical impossibility** built into the system's architecture.

**The Simple Version:**

The world needed dollars for two purposes:
1. **Transaction demand**: Settling international trade
2. **Reserve demand**: Central banks holding dollars as backing for their currencies

Both required the U.S. to run balance-of-payments deficits (export more dollars than import). But the dollar's gold convertibility depended on deficits remaining small enough that gold reserves could cover dollar liabilities. These requirements contradicted each other.

**The Math That Doesn't Work:**

Start with simplified numbers to show the impossibility:

**Year 1 (1944):**
- World trade requires $100 billion in liquidity annually
- Central banks want to hold $200 billion in reserves (2x trade volume)
- Total dollar demand: $300 billion
- U.S. gold reserves: $20 billion (actual 1944 figure: $20-24 billion)
- Gold convertibility promise: Every dollar redeemable at $35/ounce

**The Problem:**
- To supply $300 billion, U.S. must run cumulative deficits totaling $300 billion
- But gold reserves of $20 billion can only back $20 billion of dollars at full convertibility
- Ratio: $300 billion liabilities / $20 billion reserves = **15:1 leverage**

**This could work IF:**
- Foreign holders never actually convert dollars to gold
- Convertibility is a promise never tested
- Confidence remains perfect indefinitely

**But consider growth:**

**Year 10 (1954):**
- World trade has grown 50% (it actually grew faster)
- Dollar demand: $450 billion
- U.S. gold reserves: Still $20-24 billion (gold mining is slow)
- Ratio: **18.75:1 leverage**

**Year 20 (1964):**
- World trade doubled
- Dollar demand: $600 billion
- U.S. gold reserves: Actually declining (France converting dollars to gold)
- Ratio: **25:1+ leverage**

**Why This Was STRUCTURALLY Unsolvable:**

**Option A: Stop running deficits**
- Result: Dollar shortage globally

**Option B: Keep running deficits**
- Result: Dollar liabilities exceed gold reserves

- Accelerates crisis rather than solving it

France tested it in 1965-1968. Britain tested it in August 1971. The system broke because it was designed with an internal contradiction. Triffin showed this in 1959. Nixon confirmed it in 1971.

- World needs dollar liquidity (same as Bretton Woods)
- U.S. must run deficits to supply it (same)
- Persistent deficits raise questions about dollar stability (similar concern, no gold backing)
- No gold convertibility to break, but Triffin's logic still applies

### The Breaking Was Inevitable

Nixon's August 15, 1971 announcement closing the gold window was presented as temporary—a brief suspension to address speculative attacks. Markets knew immediately it was permanent. Historians know why: the Bretton Woods system had been dying since its birth.

**The System's Three Fatal Flaws:**

**Flaw 1: The Triffin Dilemma Was Unsolvable**
- World needed growing dollar supply for trade and reserves
- Dollar convertibility required limiting dollar supply
- Mathematics made both impossible simultaneously
- Triffin proved this in 1959; it took 12 years to play out

**Flaw 2: Asymmetric Adjustment Favored Surplus Countries**
- Deficit countries forced to deflate (Britain 1967)
- Surplus countries faced no penalties (France, Germany hoarded gold)
- System rewarded mercantilism, punished growth
- Recipe for persistent imbalances

**Flaw 3: Capital Mobility Rendered Fixed Rates Unworkable**
- 1944 system assumed capital controls would hold
- By 1960s, financial flows overwhelmed controls
- Speculators could test promises faster than central banks could defend them
- Technology (telecommunications) and deregulation made capital controls obsolete

**The Miracle: It Lasted 27 Years**

The surprise isn't that Bretton Woods collapsed in 1971. The surprise is it lasted until 1971. Three factors bought time:

1. **American gold reserves** ($20-24 billion in 1944) took decades to deplete
2. **Soviet threat** created political pressure to maintain Western monetary unity
3. **Bretton Woods generation** (central bankers who designed system) managed it with restraint

But by 1971, all three factors exhausted:
- Gold reserves down to $10 billion
- Vietnam War divided West, reduced cooperation
- New generation questioned why America should sacrifice for system stability

**The Question for Our Era:**

The pure fiat dollar standard that emerged after 1971 perpetuates Triffin's logic without gold convertibility to break:
- World still needs dollars for trade and reserves
- U.S. still must run deficits to supply them
- Persistent deficits still raise questions about dollar stability
- No automatic breaking point (no gold redemption to exhaust)

How long can this continue? Keynes's 1944 bancor proposal solved these problems through supranational currency and symmetric adjustment. The same solution exists today—SDRs could evolve into bancor-like reserve asset. But American power still resists surrendering privilege.

History suggests: Only crisis forces change. Bretton Woods died because contradictions became unbearable, not because policymakers chose wisely. The current system shows similar strains—persistent trade deficits, rising debt, dollar weaponization, China's accumulation creating modern Triffin trap.

The lesson of 1944-1971: Systems designed to preserve dominant power rather than systemic stability eventually break. The question is whether we'll design better before crisis forces it upon us—or repeat history's pattern of learning through catastrophe.
