On a spring morning in 1717, Isaac Newton—74 years old, Master of the Royal Mint for twenty years—stood in his private laboratory at the Tower of London conducting an assay. The room smelled of quicksilver and coal smoke. On the workbench before him lay samples of gold and silver coins, crucibles, scales precise to a fraction of a grain, and sheets of calculations covered in his meticulous hand.

Newton's task was deceptively simple: determine the proper ratio between gold and silver that would stabilize England's bimetallic currency. Since the recoinage of 1696, both metals circulated as money, with the guinea (gold coin) valued at 21 shillings (silver equivalent). But the market ratio—what merchants actually paid when trading gold for silver—had shifted due to changing global supplies.

**The calculation:**

Newton determined that the guinea's gold content, when compared to silver at market prices, was worth 21 shillings and 6 pence—not the official 21 shillings. His recommendation to the Treasury: raise the guinea's official value to 21/6 to align with market reality.

[INSERT FROM RESEARCH: Newton's actual calculations or correspondence if available]

The Treasury adopted his recommendation on December 22, 1717. The guinea was revalued to 21 shillings 6 pence—a change of approximately **2.38%** that seemed minor at the time.

What Newton expected: The adjustment would **preserve** bimetallism by preventing merchants from arbitraging the difference between official and market ratios. Gold and silver would both continue circulating as money, with the ratio now reflecting market reality.

What Newton got: Within decades, silver disappeared from England entirely, replaced by gold. The 2.38% adjustment had accidentally made gold relatively undervalued at the Mint (official price) compared to its market price abroad. Silver became overvalued. Gresham's Law—"bad money drives out good"—did the rest. Merchants exported silver (worth more abroad) and imported gold (worth more in England). By 1774, England was on a de facto gold standard.

**The irony:** History's greatest scientific mind—the man who explained planetary motion, invented calculus, and revolutionized physics—couldn't predict that a 2.38% adjustment would restructure global monetary systems for two centuries. His calculation was mathematically correct. His prediction of consequences was catastrophically wrong.

Newton died in 1727 believing he had stabilized bimetallism. Instead, he had accidentally created the gold standard that would dominate the next 200 years, shape the world economy, and ultimately collapse in depression and war. The story of money is full of such accidents—often with far greater consequences than anyone intended.

### The Unintended Consequences of Precision

Isaac Newton spent his career pursuing perfect understanding. At the Royal Mint, he applied that same rigor: prosecuting counterfeiters with ruthless efficiency, conducting assays with unprecedented precision, calculating ratios to fractions of a grain. The 1717 revaluation was Newton at his most Newtonian—mathematically exact, empirically grounded, technically flawless.

It was also completely wrong about consequences.

**What Newton calculated:** The precise ratio between gold and silver that matched current market prices.
**What Newton predicted:** Stabilization of bimetallic currency, with both metals continuing to circulate.
**What Newton created:** A 200-year monetary regime based on a single metal, shaping empire, war, and global trade.

The irony cuts deep. Newton had revolutionized physics by understanding that tiny forces—gravity's pull, planetary perturbations, orbital mechanics—accumulate into massive consequences over time. A 2.38% miscalculation in currency ratio seems trivial. But **compounded across millions of transactions over decades, tiny incentives shift entire monetary systems**.

[INSERT FROM RESEARCH: Did Newton write anything about monetary policy expectations?]
- Any letters discussing what he expected from the 1717 change?
- Did he anticipate merchants would arbitrage small differences?
- Was there any contemporary criticism of the revaluation?

By the time Newton died in 1727, silver was already draining from England. Within 50 years, the process was complete. Within 100 years, nearly every major economy had adopted gold standards, directly or indirectly following England's accidental example. The 1717 revaluation—a bureaucratic adjustment to bring official ratios in line with market prices—became the pivot point of monetary history.

**The lesson:** Monetary systems are path-dependent. Small choices compound. Accidents become institutions. And sometimes the smartest person in the room—even Isaac Newton—can't predict how markets will respond to seemingly minor adjustments.

This matters for modern policy. When central bankers adjust interest rates by 0.25%, when legislatures change tax rates by 1%, when regulators tweak capital requirements—they're making Newtonian calculations, precise and technical. The second-order consequences, playing out over years and millions of economic actors, often surprise everyone.

The gold standard wasn't planned. It was an accident—one that would dominate the next two centuries and collapse only through catastrophic war and depression. Chapter 9 will examine that collapse. But first, we need to understand how the accidental gold standard actually worked in practice.
