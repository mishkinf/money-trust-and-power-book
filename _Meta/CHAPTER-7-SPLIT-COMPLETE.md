**New Chapter Summary**:
> The Bank Restriction Act of 1797 suspended gold convertibility for 24 years, proving paper money could function without metal backing if institutions were credible. Britain won the Napoleonic Wars using fiat currency, but the 1821 return to gold at pre-war parity caused devastating deflation: note circulation contracted 92%, wheat prices fell 58%, farmers faced insolvency. The Bullionist Controversy established new orthodoxy that taxes must fund spending—a radical departure from 1694 understanding. Henry Thornton's 1802 work anticipated modern monetary theory but was ignored. The Real Bills Doctrine, despite being "wholly erroneous," survived 150 years and contributed to the Great Depression. The Bank Charter Act of 1844 tried to end fractional reserve banking by controlling note issuance but failed because Currency School economists didn't understand that bank deposits function identically to notes—banks simply shifted from creating notes to creating deposits. The transformation was complete: within 50 years of Adam Smith's statement that taxation gives value to paper money, such ideas became unthinkable. Creditor class interests manufactured orthodoxy protecting their wealth through deflation. Scottish "free banking" (1716-1845) proved competitive private banking works well within state frameworks, not independently of them. Colonial hut taxes empirically demonstrated taxation drives currency adoption through force, even as London economists denied the principle. The first breaking revealed that gold was constraint, not foundation—but the lesson would be forgotten and relearned repeatedly.

## Chapter 8: The Final Breaking (1914-1971)

**New Opening**:
> A century after Britain's return to gold in 1821, the system that had seemed eternal began to crack. World War I forced suspensions across Europe. The interwar attempt to restore the gold standard failed catastrophically. By 1971, the last link between major currencies and precious metal would be severed—not through careful planning but through crisis and necessity. The final breaking would reveal what the first had hinted: gold had never been the foundation of monetary value. Trust was.

**Content**:

- Bretton Woods conference (1944)
- Keynes vs. White (bancor vs. dollar standard)
- Triffin dilemma
- London Gold Pool
- Nixon Shock (August 15, 1971)
- Liberation from gold

**Existing Chapter Summary** (retained):
> The gold standard broke in three stages: 1797 (Bank Restriction Act suspended convertibility during Napoleonic Wars), 1914-1925 (World War I forced suspension, disastrous return at pre-war parity), and 1971 (Nixon closed gold window permanently). Each break revealed gold's limitations. The 1797-1821 suspension proved paper money could function without metal backing if institutions were credible—Bank of England notes circulated successfully for 24 years. The 1821 return to gold at pre-war parity caused devastating deflation: note circulation contracted 92%, wheat prices fell 60%, farmers faced insolvency. Churchill's 1925 return at $4.86 (10% overvalued) triggered the 1926 General Strike affecting 1.7 million workers and forced miners to accept 13% wage cuts. The gold standard imposed deflationary bias, redistributed wealth from debtors to creditors, and constrained policy responses to economic crises. Nixon's 1971 decision ended the system permanently, proving fiat money could work if institutions were credible. The lesson: money is institutional, not commodity-based—trust matters more than metal.
