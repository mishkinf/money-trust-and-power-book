# Senior Developmental Editorial Evaluation
## Money, Trust, and Power: A History of Money from Ancient Credit to Modern Fiat

**Evaluator Role:** Senior Developmental Editor & Acquisitions-Level Reviewer

## ACQUISITIONS VERDICT: **CONDITIONAL YES**

This manuscript has the intellectual architecture and narrative ambition to succeed at a major publishing house (Penguin, Harper, etc.). The core argument is original, well-researched, and challenges widespread misconceptions in ways that feel both revelatory and timely.

**Strengths:**
- Original thesis: the "great forgetting" of how money actually works
- Strong narrative arc from ancient credit to modern fiat
- Excellent human vignettes (Backwell, Reynolds, McKenzie)
- Sophisticated handling of complex economics
- Clear contemporary relevance

- Middle section (Chapters 6-8) loses momentum

## I. THE PITCH

> "What if everything you know about money is backwards? This book reveals that the 'common sense' about government budgets and fiscal responsibility is manufactured ideology from the 1820s-1840s, designed to serve creditor interests. Five thousand years of history—from Mesopotamian grain credits to the 2008 crisis—proves that money works through institutional trust and taxation, not gold or commodity backing. Understanding this transforms debates about deficits, MMT, and cryptocurrency from technical disputes into questions about power."

**Comparable titles:** Graeber's *Debt* (but more focused), Ahamed's *Lords of Finance* (but broader scope), Martin's *Money* (but clearer policy implications)

**Market:** Educated general readers interested in economics, history, policy. Strong course adoption potential.

---

## II. NARRATIVE STRUCTURE

### What Works

**Four-part arc is sound:**
1. **Part I:** Credit preceded commodity (establishes institutional foundation)
2. **Part II:** Bank of England solved credibility problem (shows how it works)
3. **Part III:** Gold standard's success created amnesia (the tragedy)
4. **Part IV:** Three breakings revealed truth (the vindication)

**The "great forgetting" (Chapter 6, lines 145-171) is brilliant:** This is the book's most original contribution—showing how 18th-century understanding of money creation was deliberately erased by 19th-century creditor-class ideology.

### What Needs Work

**1. The "So What?" arrives too late**
- Contemporary relevance is in "Why This Matters Now" sections at chapter ends
- Should be integrated throughout, not afterthoughts
- **Fix:** Open each chapter with contemporary resonance, then flashback to history

**2. Part III sags (Chapters 6-8)**
- Densest, least accessible section
- Too much exposition, not enough narrative drive
- **Fix:** Add human stories (Thomas Attwood's marginalization needs to be a character arc, not just citations)

**3. Chapter 11 tries to do too much**
- 262 lines covering 53 years (1971-2024)
- Volcker, 2008, MMT, cryptocurrency, COVID—all rushed
- **Fix:** Choose 2-3 topics, develop fully

## III. ACCESSIBILITY ISSUES

### Jargon Problems

**Terms used before definition:**
- "Specie" (Ch 2, line 17) - never defined
- "Fiscal capacity" (Ch 3, line 50) - defined much later
- "Sterilizing gold inflows" (Ch 9, line 46) - never defined
- "Procyclical policy" (Ch 7, line 97) - definition after use

**Fix:** Define at first use with simple language: "When gold flowed into America, the Federal Reserve 'sterilized' the inflows—prevented the gold from creating new money."

### Assumed Knowledge

**Book assumes readers know:**
- What Napoleonic Wars were
- Why Great Depression matters
- What Bretton Woods was
- What 2008 crisis involved

**Fix:** Add brief contextual paragraphs: "The Napoleonic Wars (1803-1815) were Europe's first total war, requiring unprecedented government spending."

### Numbers Need Context

**Examples without scale:**
- "£1.2 million" - Is this big?
- "92% contraction" - What does this feel like?

**Fix:** "£1.2 million—roughly 100% of annual government revenue" or "Note circulation contracted 92%—imagine if 92% of money in your bank account vanished."

## IV. CHAPTER-BY-CHAPTER NOTES

### PREFACE (75 lines)
- **Strength:** Opening anecdote (1797) establishes stakes immediately
- **Weakness:** Too long for major publisher (want 3-5 pages max)
- **Fix:** Cut "Six Essential Themes" (redundant with Part Intros), aim for 50 lines

### PART I: ORIGINS

**Ch 1: Birth of Credit**
- Strong: Honest treatment of metallist challenge (Regression Theorem)
- Weak: Too many concepts too fast
- Fix: Add simple metaphor: "Money as language—grammar (unit of account) before words (currency)"

**Ch 2: Merchants of Trust**
- Strong: Amsterdam Wisselbank section
- Weak: Medieval to modern transition rushed
- Fix: "When you swipe your card in Paris, you're using the mechanism Florentine merchants invented in 1450"

**Ch 3: Sovereign's Dilemma**
- Strong: Edward Backwell story is gripping
- Weak: Domestic vs. foreign credit distinction gets lost
- Fix: Simple frame: "At home, kings create money by decree. Abroad, they're just another debtor."

### PART II: INSTITUTIONAL REVOLUTION

**Ch 4: Private Money Failed (70 lines)**
- Strong: Concise, focused
- Weak: Too short compared to others
- Fix: Either expand or merge with Ch 5

**Ch 5: Bank of England Solution**
- Strong: Money multiplication explanation (lines 52-78) is brilliantly clear
- Weak: Two-tier system assumes readers remember earlier material
- Fix: "Picture a pyramid: gold at bottom, BoE notes middle, country bank notes top"

### PART III: GOLD STANDARD ERA (NEEDS MOST WORK)

**Ch 6: Newton's Accident**
- Strong: Opening scene vivid; "Great Forgetting" section is intellectual climax
- Weak: Overstuffed—Newton biography, Gresham's Law, gold points, crises, AND forgetting
- Fix: Split into two chapters to give "great forgetting" the space it deserves

**Ch 7: Paper Proves Itself**
- Strong: Bank Restriction narrative excellent; human cost of 1821 deflation powerful
- Weak: Real Bills Doctrine interrupts flow
- Fix: Move Real Bills to Ch 8 (where debates belong)

**Ch 8: Battle for Monetary Truth**
- Strong: Creditor class analysis sharp; Scottish banking fair
- Weak: Reads like academic article, not narrative nonfiction
- Fix: Lead with Thomas Attwood's life story; integrate debates into that narrative

### PART IV: BREAKING AND BEYOND

**Ch 9: Interwar Catastrophe**
- Strong: Hugh Reynolds story (coal miner) is heartbreaking
- Weak: Covers too much (Churchill, France, Roosevelt, Bretton Woods preview)
- Fix: End with Roosevelt 1933; move Bretton Woods to Ch 10

**Ch 10: Bretton Woods**
- Strong: Keynes vs. White well-told; Triffin dilemma clear
- Weak: Feels like two essays without connective tissue
- Fix: Add transition: "The system created in 1944 contained seeds of its own destruction"

**Ch 11: Age of Pure Fiat (262 lines)**
- Strong: 2008 narrative excellent; Maureen McKenzie story essential; MMT section fair
- Weak: Too long, covers too much (1971-2024), rushed despite length
- Fix: Choose one focus—either historical (1971-2008) OR contemporary policy manifesto

### EPILOGUE (153 lines)
- Weak: Summarizes lessons already clear
- Fix: Either expand with provocative future vision OR cut entirely, end with Ch 11

## V. ESSENTIAL CHANGES (TIER 1)

### 1. Restructure Part III
- Current structure buries "great forgetting" in densest section
- Split Ch 6 into two chapters
- Give intellectual transformation argument the prominence it deserves

### 2. Add Human Stories (Need 3-5 More)
- Ch 1: Mesopotamian farmer with temple debt
- Ch 2: Florentine merchant using bills of exchange
- Ch 6: Bank of England clerk during crisis
- **Ch 8: Thomas Attwood's life (ESSENTIAL)**
- Ch 10: American family during Nixon Shock

### 3. Integrate Contemporary Relevance
- Don't relegate to chapter endings
- Open each chapter with modern scene, flashback to history
- Example: Ch 7 opens with Fed creating trillions in 2008, then: "This wasn't unprecedented. In 1797..."

### 4. Cut 15-20% (Target 80,000-85,000 words)
- Tighten technical sections
- Move some material to footnotes/appendix
- Cut redundancies between preface/part intros/epilogue

### 5. Define Terms at First Use
- Create glossary
- But more importantly: define inline with simple language
- Never assume reader knows technical terms

## VIII. FINAL ASSESSMENT

### Would I Acquire This Book?

**YES—with significant developmental editing.**

This manuscript has the intellectual substance and narrative ambition to succeed at a major house. The core argument is original and important. The research is solid. The writing is often excellent.

But it's not ready yet. The accessibility issues will lose general readers. The pacing problems will cause abandonment in the middle. The length needs trimming.

### What It Needs to Succeed

**Essential (must be done):**
1. Restructure Part III to foreground "great forgetting"
2. Add 3-5 more human vignettes
3. Integrate contemporary relevance throughout
4. Cut 15-20% for pacing
5. Define all terms at first use

**Highly recommended:**
1. Add visual metaphors for abstract concepts
2. Strengthen transitions
3. Contextualize all numbers
4. Expand "aha" moments

**Timeline:** 4-6 months of developmental editing, then ready for copyediting.
