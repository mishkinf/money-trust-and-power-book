### Chapter 4: The Glorious Revolution and the Birth of the Bank of England

**New Section Added: "The world before central banks"** (after opening, before "The cost of legitimacy")

This section provides essential context that was missing:

1. **Goldsmith-Bankers (1680s)**
   - Private arrangements with individual receipts
   - No unified currency or lender of last resort
   - Fatal weakness exposed by Stop of the Exchequer (1672)
   - Names: Edward Backwell, Robert Vyner

2. **International Context**
   - Sweden's Riksbank (1668) - first central bank
   - Amsterdam's Bank of 1609 - public bank model
   - Shows England was behind in institutional innovation

3. **Why It Matters**
   - Demonstrates the Bank of England was revolutionary, not evolutionary
   - Explains why parliamentary backing was necessary
   - Sets up the contrast between private credit and sovereign-backed institutions

**New Section Added: "The evolution toward central banking"** (after "triangle of trust", before "legacy of 1694")

This section explains how the Bank evolved beyond its initial purpose:

1. **Three Key Functions Developed**
   - **Monopoly on note issuance**: Replaced chaotic goldsmith notes with uniform currency
   - **Lender of last resort**: Emergency liquidity during panics (Thornton 1802, Bagehot 1873)
   - **Manager of government debt**: Fiscal agent for the state

2. **International Influence**
   - **Banque de France (1800)**: Napoleon explicitly modeled on Bank of England
   - **Federal Reserve (1913)**: Drew on English precedent after chaotic free banking
   - Established template for hybrid public-private central banks

3. **Historical Significance**
   - Not the first (Sweden's Riksbank was 26 years earlier)
   - But first to prove the model could survive war, crisis, and political upheaval
   - Demonstrated that credibility > metal reserves

## Key Concepts Now Properly Explained

### 1. Pre-Central Banking Fragmentation
- Multiple private issuers (goldsmiths)
- No unified currency
- No systemic stability mechanism
- Vulnerable to sovereign defaults

### 2. Central Banking Innovation
- Monopoly note issuance → uniform currency
- Lender of last resort → systemic stability
- Government fiscal agent → credible public debt

### 3. International Diffusion
- Sweden (1668) → England (1694) → France (1800) → USA (1913)
- Model spread because it worked
- Required constitutional foundations (parliamentary control)

### 4. War Finance Connection
- Central banks created to finance wars
- Bank of England: Nine Years' War (1688-1697)
- Banque de France: Napoleonic Wars
- Federal Reserve: WWI preparedness
