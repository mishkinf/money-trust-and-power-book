## 1. BOOK PROMISE & CORE QUESTIONS

### Reader Contract (3-5 sentences)

If you read this book, you will understand: (1) Why money has value—not because "everyone believes" or gold backing, but because states create demand through taxation and institutions maintain credibility; (2) How money actually works—states can issue liabilities, taxation makes them non-optional, private actors cannot net-create state money; (3) Why "taxes fund spending" is backwards—historically, states spent first and taxed later to create currency demand; (4) When and why gold became the story—through Newton's 1717 accident, sustained by institutional credibility not metal's value, abandoned when flexibility became necessary; (5) How we got trapped in bad metaphors—the gold standard's success (1821-1914) obscured earlier understandings, making explicit 1694-1776 knowledge nearly unthinkable by the 19th century.

### Core Questions Answered

1. **If the state can issue money, why tax?** → Create currency demand, control inflation, organize power—not primarily "raise revenue"
2. **When did private banks create money?** → Always, but within state frameworks—goldsmith receipts to country banks to modern deposits require state foundation
3. **How did gold become the story?** → Newton's accidental 1717 revaluation, sustained by British credibility, globalized 1870-1914, mistaken for foundation rather than consequence
4. **Why do people believe the wrong story?** → Gold standard success buried earlier knowledge; creditor class manufactured orthodoxy (1821-1844); textbooks taught false models for convenience
5. **Domestic vs foreign money difference?** → Domestic: taxation creates demand. Foreign: institutional credibility enables conversion. Tally sticks worked in England not France because English tax collectors accepted them

## 5. TAX/SPEND FRAMING: EXPLICIT RECOMMENDATION

### Where to State the Correction

**RECOMMENDATION: Seed it, then prove it through history**

The book currently does this well but could be MORE EXPLICIT in three places:

**1. Preface (after line 18, "Second" theme)**:  
Current: "Taxation drives currency demand. Fiat money has value because governments require taxes be paid in it..."  
**Add**: "This inverts the common story. We're told governments must tax or borrow before spending, as if they need to 'find the money.' But historically, sovereigns spent first—issuing tallies, notes, credits—and taxed later to create demand for what they'd issued. The sequence matters: **spend first, tax later** is how money enters circulation. The 'taxes fund spending' story is backwards."

**2. Chapter 5 (line 50, after taxation cycle)**:  
Current cycle explanation is good.  
**Add**: "This reveals the fundamental inversion in public discourse. Politicians claim 'we're out of money' and 'must raise taxes to afford programs.' But the government creates money by spending—the Bank's notes entered circulation through government expenditure, not through prior taxation. Taxes don't fund spending; **taxes create demand for what government spends**. The constraint is not finance but real resources: inflation occurs when spending exceeds productive capacity, not when it exceeds tax revenue."

**3. Chapter 8 (lines 156-160, "real constraints" section)**:  
Current explanation of productive capacity is excellent.  
**Add explicit contrast**: "This is why the household budget metaphor fails. Households must earn before spending—they cannot create money. Governments that issue their own currency can spend before taxing—they create money by spending. The constraint for households is finance (income). The constraint for currency-issuing governments is real resources (productive capacity). Confusing these leads to policy disasters: imposing austerity during recessions when resources sit idle, claiming scarcity when the only limit is political will."

### Justification

**Front-loading** (Preface + early chapters) risks seeming polemical before the historical evidence is presented.  
**Closing reframing** (Epilogue only) risks reader missing the point until the end.  
**Seeding then proving** (Preface states it, Chapters 3-5 prove it historically, Chapter 8 applies it) is optimal—gives reader the framework, demonstrates it through evidence, applies it to modern context.

## 6. NON-NEGOTIABLES (7 Things Book Must Make Explicit)

1. **Taxes → demand, not revenue**: State spending creates money; taxation creates demand for it. Sequence: spend first, tax later. (Preface, Ch5, Ch8)

2. **Domestic vs external constraint**: Domestic money works through taxation (tally sticks in England). Foreign money requires institutional credibility (gold for foreign creditors). (Ch3, Ch5)

3. **Why gold arose**: Not because intrinsically valuable, but as neutral collateral for international settlement when domestic tax authority didn't reach. Newton's accident, sustained by British credibility. (Ch6)

4. **When private banks entered**: Always created money (goldsmiths, country banks, modern deposits) but within state frameworks—charters, settlement in state money, lender of last resort. (Ch2, Ch4, Ch8)

5. **Why people believe wrong story**: Gold standard success (1821-1914) obscured earlier knowledge; creditor class manufactured orthodoxy; textbooks taught false models (money multiplier) for convenience. (Ch6, Ch7, Ch8)

6. **Real constraints are productive capacity**: Government can create money without limit, but inflation occurs when spending exceeds productive capacity. Constraint is real resources (labor, capital), not finance. (Ch8)

7. **Institutional credibility matters more than metal**: Bank Restriction 1797-1821 proved paper works without gold. 2008 proved fiat flexibility prevents depressions. Trust in institutions, not faith in metal, makes money work. (Throughout, synthesized in Epilogue)
