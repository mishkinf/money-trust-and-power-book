# Book Organization Guide

## Structure

This book is organized into numbered folders to maintain chronological order:

- `01-Preface/` - Introduction and framing
- `02-Chapter-1/` - Origins of Money as Credit and Obligation
- `03-Chapter-2/` - Merchants of Trust: The Rise of the Credit Network
- `04-Chapter-3/` - Monarchs, War, and the Invention of the Fiscal State
- `05-Chapter-4/` - The Glorious Revolution and the Birth of the Bank of England
- `06-Chapter-5/` - How the Bank Created Money (and Why Taxes Made It Believable)
- `07-Chapter-6/` - Gold, Empire, and the Globalization of Trust
- `08-Chapter-7/` - Collapse, Bretton Woods, and the Fiat Era
- `09-Chapter-8/` - The Modern Legacy: Trust Without Metal
- `10-Epilogue/` - From Gold to Governance
- `_Meta/` - Organizational files, indices, and source mappings
- `Research/` - Source materials and research documents

## File Naming Convention

Within each chapter folder:
- `01-main.md` - Main chapter content
- `02-section-name.md` - Additional sections (if chapter is split)
- `sources.md` - Detailed source notes and citations

## Key Concepts Tracked

The book traces six essential themes:

1. **The Nature of Royal Debt** - What monarchs borrowed and how
2. **Limits of Domestic Credit** - Why tally sticks couldn't cross borders
3. **War Goods and Foreign Settlement** - What debt purchased and why it required metal
4. **Coinage Mandates** - How states created demand for their currency
5. **International Trust Networks** - Evolution from merchant credit to institutional finance
6. **Institutional Transformation** - From reputation to Parliament to central banks

## Citation Style

All chapters use numbered footnotes with full sources listed at the end. Research source documents are referenced in `_Meta/source-mapping.md`.
