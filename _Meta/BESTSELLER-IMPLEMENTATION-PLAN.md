## Money and Power

This book reveals how money works by tracing 5,000 years of history. But it also reveals something else: **monetary systems are never neutral**. They distribute wealth, power, and opportunity in systematic ways.

From Mesopotamian debt jubilees to medieval land concentration to modern housing unaffordability, the pattern repeats: unchecked wealth accumulation destabilizes societies by converting productive assets into rent-extraction mechanisms. When new money flows primarily to asset owners through quantitative easing and bank lending, it inflates asset prices (housing, stocks) rather than wages, systematically advantaging those who already own over those who work.

#### 2.5 Chapter 11 Addition: Bonds as Wealth Transfer (Week 6)
**Add**: 300-400 words

**Content**:
"If government creates money by spending and doesn't need to 'borrow,' why issue bonds? The answer reveals a wealth transfer.

Government bonds offer interest-bearing assets to those with excess money—typically the wealthy. Interest payments represent transfers from the general public (taxes) to bondholders. This isn't necessary for government finance—it's a policy choice to provide safe assets.

QE made this explicit: Fed bought bonds, paying bondholders with newly created reserves. The alternative wasn't 'fiscal responsibility'—it was choosing who receives new money. QE channeled it to bondholders. Different policy could channel it elsewhere.

This isn't an argument against bonds—it's clarification of mechanism. Understanding this transforms 'debt crisis' rhetoric from moral panic to policy choice."
