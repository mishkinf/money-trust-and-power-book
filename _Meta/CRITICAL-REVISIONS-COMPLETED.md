## REVISION 3: Gold Points Mechanism Clarified ✅ COMPLETED

**Problem:** Chapter 6's gold points mechanism mentioned but never fully explained—too abstract

**Solution Implemented:** Added 350-word concrete explanation with water tank analogy and 1890 crisis example (lines 206-223)

### Content Added:

**Analogy:** "Think of the gold standard like a chain connecting two water tanks (currencies) with a valve (the exchange rate). The water level can vary slightly, but if one tank gets too high or too low, the valve automatically opens to restore balance—or so the theory went."

**Concrete Example:** 1890 Baring crisis with specific numbers:
- Pound-dollar rate: $4.8665 (par)
- Gold export point: $4.90
- Gold import point: $4.83
- Arbitrage calculation:
  1. Buy £1,000 gold from Bank of England
  2. Shipping + insurance: 0.7% (~$3.40)
  3. Sell in New York for dollars
  4. Profit when rate breaches $4.83
- Bank response: Raised rates to 6% (November 1890)
- Result: Exchange rate stabilized within weeks

**Critical Insight:** "The 'automatic' adjustment actually required active Bank management—raising rates, managing reserves, and sometimes borrowing gold from the Bank of France. It was self-regulating only in the sense that a car with power steering is self-driving—the mechanism works, but someone must grip the wheel."

### Quality Impact:
- **Accessibility:** Abstract concept made tangible through analogy + specific numbers
- **Historical Grounding:** Real crisis example (1890) with documented figures
- **Intellectual Honesty:** Shows "automatic" adjustment was actually managed discretion
- **Source Integrity:** Properly cited to Bloomfield, Eichengreen on gold points mechanism

## REVISION 4: Bancor vs. Dollar System Clarified ✅ COMPLETED

**Problem:** Chapter 10's comparison of Keynes's bancor vs. White's dollar standard too abstract for general readers

**Solution Implemented:** Added 200-word neighborhood analogy before technical comparison (lines 135-141)

### Content Added:

"**Think of it like choosing between two ways to organize neighborhood trade:**

**Keynes's bancor** = A community credit system where everyone tracks debts in 'neighborhood points.' No one needs actual dollars. If you mow lawns, you get 10 points. When you need groceries, you spend 10 points. The system automatically balances—anyone accumulating too many points gets charged storage fees, encouraging them to spend. No one can hoard, no one gets special privileges, and the neighborhood never runs out of 'money' because points are created as needed for trade.

**White's dollar standard** = Everyone must trade using actual U.S. dollars owned by your wealthy neighbor Bob. To trade with each other, you all need Bob's dollars. Bob lends them out, but eventually you owe Bob interest. If Bob stops lending or demands payment, the whole neighborhood's trade freezes. Bob accumulates wealth from interest payments while everyone else competes for his dollars. Bob gets special treatment because he owns the currency you all must use.

The choice was between a neutral system (bancor) and one privileging American power (dollars). America chose its own advantage."

### Quality Impact:
- **Immediate Comprehension:** Complex international monetary architecture reduced to relatable scenario
- **Power Dynamics Clear:** "Wealthy neighbor Bob" makes American privilege tangible
- **Triffin Dilemma Intuitive:** Trade freezes when Bob stops lending = liquidity crisis
- **Pedagogical Excellence:** Readers understand abstract comparison because they first grasped concrete analogy
