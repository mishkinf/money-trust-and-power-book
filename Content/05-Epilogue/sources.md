# Epilogue Sources

### **Notes**

1. Venezuela oil production and hyperinflation timeline data from research synthesis showing production decline from 3.2M bpd (2013) to 700,000 bpd (2020), with inflation acceleration beginning 2013-2016 before heavy monetary expansion. See Rodriguez, F. (2022). "Sanctions and the Venezuelan Economy," *Latin American Economic Review*.

2. Weimar Germany mark stabilization and Ruhr occupation impact documented in research showing mark at 320:1 through mid-1922, with hyperinflation erupting only after January 1923 Ruhr occupation eliminating 80% of coal/steel production.

3. Japan debt statistics: 250-260% debt-to-GDP, Bank of Japan holdings exceeding 50% of JGBs, deflation averaging -0.3% annually (1995-2013), inflation only 0.5% (2013-2020) despite unprecedented QE. Data from Bank of Japan official statistics and Ministry of Finance reports.

4. Federal Reserve balance sheet expansion from $900 billion (2008) to $8.9 trillion (March 2022) documented in Federal Reserve H.4.1 reports "Factors Affecting Reserve Balances." M2 growth of 27% (2020-2021) from Federal Reserve Economic Data (FRED).

5. Bank Restriction Act period (1797-1821) data showing 24-year suspension, 226% debt-to-GDP ratio, and 22.3% total inflation over 18 years (averaging 1.2% annually). See Clapham, J. (1945). *The Bank of England: A History, Vol. 2, 1797–1914.* Cambridge University Press.

6. Argentina 2018-2019 IMF loan of $57 billion (largest in IMF history), 55-57% foreign currency debt, 100% peso devaluation, 60-75% interest rates, and poverty increase from 27% to 35%. Data from IMF reports and Argentine national statistics.

7. Japan-Argentina debt comparison: Japan 260% debt-to-GDP (100% yen-denominated, 88% domestically held) versus Argentina 75% average debt-to-GDP with 60%+ foreign currency exposure. Nine Argentine crises since 1980 versus zero Japanese defaults. See research synthesis on currency sovereignty.

8. US current account deficit ($819 billion in 2023) and foreign Treasury holdings ($9.05 trillion) from US Treasury "Report on Foreign Portfolio Holdings of US Securities" and Bureau of Economic Analysis data. "Exorbitant privilege" calculation of 46-50 basis point interest rate suppression from Federal Reserve research.

9. Dollar reserve share decline from 63.3% (2010) to 57.8% (Q4 2024) from IMF COFER (Currency Composition of Official Foreign Exchange Reserves) database. Dollar involvement in 88% of FX transactions from BIS Triennial Survey (2022).

10. Eichengreen, B. (1992). *Golden Fetters: The Gold Standard and the Great Depression, 1919–1939.* Oxford University Press. Establishes that countries abandoning gold earlier recovered faster with less severe output contractions and lower unemployment.

### **Further Reading**

For readers interested in exploring these themes further, the following works provide deeper analysis:

**On ancient monetary systems:**
- Hudson, M. (2018). *...and forgive them their debts: Lending, Foreclosure and Redemption from Bronze Age Finance to the Jubilee Year.* ISLET.
- Graeber, D. (2011). *Debt: The First 5,000 Years.* Melville House.

**On the evolution of banking:**
- Kindleberger, C. P. (1978). *Manias, Panics, and Crashes: A History of Financial Crises.* Basic Books.
- Ferguson, N. (2008). *The Ascent of Money: A Financial History of the World.* Penguin.

**On the gold standard:**
- Eichengreen, B. (1992). *Golden Fetters: The Gold Standard and the Great Depression, 1919–1939.* Oxford University Press.
- Bordo, M. D., & Eichengreen, B. (Eds.). (1993). *A Retrospective on the Bretton Woods System.* University of Chicago Press.

**On modern monetary systems:**
- Wray, L. R. (1998). *Understanding Modern Money: The Key to Full Employment and Price Stability.* Edward Elgar.
- Mehrling, P. (2011). *The New Lombard Street: How the Fed Became the Dealer of Last Resort.* Princeton University Press.

- Bernanke, B. S. (2015). *The Courage to Act: A Memoir of a Crisis and Its Aftermath.* W. W. Norton.
- Tooze, A. (2018). *Crashed: How a Decade of Financial Crises Changed the World.* Viking.

**On Central Bank Digital Currencies:**

The journey from ancient grain credits to modern fiat currency is long, but the principles remain constant. Money is trust, institutionalized and made portable. Understanding its history is essential to managing its future.
