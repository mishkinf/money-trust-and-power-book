Introducing Commons Currency

A proposal for a new global currency—created, governed and continuously refined by a truly democratic assembly of representatives drawn from every region of the world.

We're building an open, participatory process to select and empower delegates, debate monetary rules, and ratify a global monetary union designed to serve people (not states or elites). Join us as we grow a worldwide body to define and steward the first democratically governed global currency.

The Opportunity

Humanity possesses unprecedented resources, technology, and knowledge to solve our greatest challenges—climate change, healthcare access, infrastructure needs, economic security. We have idle workers ready to build, unused factories capable of producing, and unmet needs waiting to be addressed. What we lack is a monetary system that enables coordinated action without forcing nations into impossible choices.

The current system creates artificial constraints: nations with dollar-denominated debt face devastating choices between crushing domestic growth or accepting currency collapses. Austerity measures imposed during crises prevent countries from mobilizing their own resources to solve their own problems. This isn't inevitable—it's a design flaw we can fix.

The Solution

Commons Currency enables nations to invest in solving real problems—building infrastructure, training workers, developing clean energy, providing healthcare—without the artificial financial constraints that force unnecessary austerity. The real constraint isn't money; it's productive capacity. Commons helps nations mobilize idle resources to create genuine value.

Unlike institutions dominated by powerful economies, Commons ensures every nation has an equal say through one-nation-one-vote governance. Inspired by Keynes's Bancor proposal, it introduces balance-correction mechanisms that address trade imbalances without forcing struggling economies into harsh austerity.

Blockchain-based ledgers guarantee transparency and accountability, eliminating backroom manipulation. Nations maintain fiscal autonomy—the freedom to invest in their own priorities—while operating within collaborative guidelines that ensure global stability.

This creates a foundation for genuine cooperation: nations can pursue full employment, invest in their people, and solve shared challenges like climate change—not through zero-sum competition, but through coordinated action that benefits everyone.

Why not Bitcoin? Bitcoin is not money—it fails as a medium of exchange, unit of account, and store of value. Its deflationary design would cause economic harm, not solve problems. It has no governance to respond to crises, wastes massive energy, and doesn't address dollar hegemony, trade imbalances, or unemployment. Read the full analysis →

How It Works

Commons enables nations to issue currency for productive investment—infrastructure, education, healthcare, climate solutions—within democratic quotas based on real economic capacity. When you have unemployed workers and unmet needs, the constraint isn't money—it's organizing resources effectively. Commons provides the monetary architecture to do exactly that.

Automatic mechanisms prevent persistent trade imbalances: surplus countries systematically recycle their excess through investments into deficit nations, maintaining equilibrium without forcing austerity. This creates a virtuous cycle where surplus nations profit from productive investments while deficit nations build capacity.

Unlike current systems that sacrifice sovereignty for stability, Commons enhances both. Governments retain the freedom to invest in citizen priorities while benefiting from safeguards against speculation and manipulation. The result: nations can mobilize their resources to solve problems, create meaningful work, and build prosperity—not as an abstract promise, but as a practical reality.

Principles

  • • Money as a public good
  • • Value creation before value extraction
  • • Monetary democracy, not hegemony
  • • Inflation control without austerity
  • • Full transparency of monetary operations
  • • National fiscal autonomy with guardrails for global stability
  • • Political neutrality and global unity
  • • The right to work is a fundamental human right

Our Vision

Commons envisions a world economy built on unity, fairness, and transparency—where money serves humanity, not geopolitics or private interests. We see economic cooperation as the key to a peaceful, stable world. Our vision respects every nation's cultural diversity and domestic sovereignty while fostering solidarity through a global financial system.

Economic empowerment must be universal, accessible, and democratically governed. Commons seeks no partisan interest, only the shared good of all humanity.

Economic Rights for a Changing World

In 1944, President Franklin Roosevelt proposed a Second Bill of Rights—economic rights as fundamental as our political freedoms. As we face unprecedented disruption from automation and artificial intelligence, these principles matter more than ever.

Every person has the right to:

  • • Meaningful work that provides fair compensation and dignity
  • • Economic security protecting against forces beyond individual control
  • • Healthcare, education, and housing as foundations of a decent life
  • • Protection from monopolies and unfair economic power
  • • Freedom from fear of unemployment, old age, sickness, and destitution

As technology reshapes our economy, millions face displacement through no fault of their own. This is not a threat—it's a call to action. The same forces eliminating jobs can free humanity to address our greatest challenges: building sustainable infrastructure, advancing medical research, teaching, caring for communities, restoring our environment.

But only if we build economic systems that value human contribution beyond market wages. Commons creates the foundation for an economy where everyone can contribute meaningfully, regardless of what automation disrupts. Where your value isn't determined by whether your job survived technological change, but by what you can build, create, teach, heal, or restore.

This isn't about handouts—it's about recognizing that in a world of abundance created by technology, everyone deserves the opportunity to contribute their talents and receive fair compensation. Whether you're a factory worker facing automation, a programmer building new systems, or a retiree with decades of wisdom to share: your contribution matters, and the economy should reflect that.
