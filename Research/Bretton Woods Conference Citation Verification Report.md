## ITEM 3: Late-Night Scheduling Tactics

**STATUS: ⚠️ NEEDS SIGNIFICANT REVISION / CONFLATION OF EVENTS**

### Evidence
The specific claim that "American delegates scheduled discussions for late-night sessions when British participants were exhausted, then called for votes before opposition could organize" when Keynes proposed changes to voting weights **is not substantiated** by historical sources.

The claim conflates several real, documented events into an overstated narrative:

### Verified Elements:

**1. Keynes's Health Crisis:**
Keynes collapsed on hotel stairs at Bretton Woods on July 19, 1944, suffering a heart attack from overwork and exhaustion.

**Chicago Citation:**
Skidelsky, Robert. *John Maynard Keynes: Fighting for Britain, 1937–1946*. Vol. 3 of *John Maynard Keynes*. London: Macmillan, 2000.

**2. Rushed Final Proceedings:**
Conference extended from July 19 to July 22; delegates signed without adequate review time (see Keynes quote in Item 1).

**3. American Procedural Control:**
Harry Dexter White explicitly stated his tactical intent: "The importance of why we need somebody [White himself] to chairman this, who knows the complete matter is that he should prevent coming to a vote on matters which he doesn't wish to come to a vote on, and in general arranging the discussion in such a way that we are never caught with an agreement among the Commission on something we don't want."

**Chicago Citation:**
White, Harry Dexter. Statement to Henry Morgenthau Jr., Bretton Woods Conference, July 1944. Quoted in Benn Steil, *The Battle of Bretton Woods: John Maynard Keynes, Harry Dexter White, and the Making of a New World Order* (Princeton, NJ: Princeton University Press, 2013), 212.

**4. Conference Procedures:**
The conference "generally proceeded by negotiation and informal consensus rather than by formal voting." Voting quotas were resolved through bilateral negotiations throughout the conference, not in specific late-night sessions.

### What Was NOT Found:
- Evidence of deliberately scheduled late-night sessions to exploit British exhaustion
- Timing of such tactics relative to Keynes's voting weight proposals
- Calls for votes "before opposition could organize" (White's strategy was preventing votes, not rushing them)

### Recommended Revision:

**Original (problematic):** "When Keynes proposed significant changes to voting weights, American delegates scheduled discussions for late-night sessions when British participants were exhausted, then called for votes before opposition could organize."

**Revised (accurate):** "American delegates, led by Harry Dexter White, exercised tight procedural control over the Bretton Woods Conference, deliberately managing when items came to votes to prevent outcomes unfavorable to U.S. interests. White stated his intent 'to prevent coming to a vote on matters which he doesn't wish to come to a vote on.' The rushed final proceedings, combined with Keynes's collapse from a heart attack on July 19, 1944, weakened the British negotiating position. Delegates ultimately signed the final agreement without adequate time to review it."

### Draft Footnote (Revised Version):

<sup>1</sup> Harry Dexter White exercised strategic control over conference proceedings, telling Treasury Secretary Henry Morgenthau Jr. that as chairman he aimed "to prevent coming to a vote on matters which he doesn't wish to come to a vote on, and in general arranging the discussion in such a way that we are never caught with an agreement among the Commission on something we don't want." The exhaustion of British delegates, culminating in Keynes's heart attack on July 19, further weakened their negotiating position. Benn Steil, *The Battle of Bretton Woods: John Maynard Keynes, Harry Dexter White, and the Making of a New World Order* (Princeton, NJ: Princeton University Press, 2013), 212, 224; Robert Skidelsky, *John Maynard Keynes: Fighting for Britain, 1937–1946*, vol. 3 of *John Maynard Keynes* (London: Macmillan, 2000).

## ITEM 5: Procedural Death of Bancor and Marshall Plan Anachronism

### CRITICAL CORRECTION CONFIRMED:

**❌ MARSHALL PLAN REFERENCE IS ANACHRONISTIC ERROR**

The Marshall Plan was announced by Secretary of State George C. Marshall at Harvard University on **June 5, 1947**—nearly **three years after** the Bretton Woods Conference (July 1-22, 1944). The Economic Recovery Act was signed by President Truman on April 3, 1948.

**Chicago Citation:**
U.S. Department of State, Office of the Historian. "Marshall Plan, 1948." *Milestones in the History of U.S. Foreign Relations*. Accessed November 7, 2025. https://history.state.gov/milestones/1945-1952/marshall-plan.

### Correct 1944 Terminology for Smaller Nations' Incentives:

**Use these verified terms:**

1. **"Access to American credit and capital"** ✅ RECOMMENDED
2. **"Dollar shortage concerns"** ✅ DOCUMENTED  
3. **"Post-war reconstruction assistance promises"** ✅ SAFE
4. **"American financial dominance"** ✅ ACCURATE
5. **"Lend-Lease continuation"** ⚠️ COMPLEX (see below)

**Best replacement language:** "access to American credit and post-war financial assistance" or "need to secure access to dollars and American capital to address anticipated dollar shortages."

**Lend-Lease Context:**
Article VII of the 1942 Lend-Lease Agreement explicitly linked U.S. wartime aid to Britain's pledge to dismantle imperial preferences and participate in post-war monetary negotiations. However, using "Lend-Lease continuation" requires careful framing since it primarily affected Britain, not smaller nations.

**Chicago Citation:**
Federal Reserve History. "Creation of the Bretton Woods System." Accessed November 7, 2025. https://www.federalreservehistory.org/essays/bretton-woods-created.

**Key Quote:**
"The overwhelming concern was that of a 'dollar shortage,' that is, European countries in particular lacked the dollars required to import food and energy necessary to prevent the collapse of their war-ravaged economies."

### Bancor/ICU Claims Verification:

**CLAIM: ICU never received formal floor vote**
**STATUS: ⚠️ NEEDS NUANCE**

The bancor/International Clearing Union was effectively abandoned **before** the Bretton Woods Conference:
- Joint Statement by Experts (April 21, 1944) already reflected compromise closer to White Plan
- Atlantic City pre-conference (June 15-30, 1944) finalized draft proposals based on White Plan
- May 25, 1944 U.S. invitation specified conference was "for the purpose of formulating definite proposals for an International Monetary Fund" (ICU not mentioned)

**Accurate statement:** The ICU never came to a vote at Bretton Woods because it had already been rejected in preliminary bilateral negotiations. The conference voted only on final approval of the IMF/IBRD agreements.

**CLAIM: "Procedural suffocation"**
**STATUS: ❌ PHRASE NOT FOUND IN SOURCES**

Alternative verified phrases: "dismissed by White as impractical," "scrapped well before the July meeting," "vetoed by the US" in bilateral negotiations, "off the Allied Powers' agenda before the Bretton Woods Conference."

**CLAIM: "Dollar" used as placeholder**
**STATUS: ⚠️ OVERSTATED**

The dollar became the reference currency through the drafting process, but it was the U.S. negotiating position from early on. Indian delegate Ardeshir Darabshaw Shroff questioned White about dollar references on July 13, 1944. The Joint Statement (April 1944) introduced "gold convertible exchange" (later "gold convertible currency"), which effectively meant dollars.

**CLAIM: Working groups discussed IMF (White plan) not ICU**
**STATUS: ✅ VERIFIED**

Conference organized in three Commissions after Atlantic City:
- Commission I: The Fund (White as President)
- Commission II: The Bank (Keynes as President)
- Commission III: Other International Financial Cooperation

The main agenda was the IMF/World Bank, not the ICU.

**CLAIM: Three-week timeline prevented consideration of alternatives**
**STATUS: ⚠️ PARTIALLY ACCURATE**

Conference lasted 21-22 days (July 1-22, 1944). However, the real constraint was that alternatives had already been eliminated in 1942-1944 bilateral negotiations. The three weeks was for finalizing details of the IMF/IBRD, not considering competing systems.

### Recommended Revision for Bancor Section:

**Original (problematic):** "Keynes's International Clearing Union (bancor) never received formal floor vote and died through 'procedural suffocation.' Dollar used as placeholder in American drafts. Working groups discussed IMF not ICU. Three-week timeline prevented consideration of alternatives. Smaller nations supported this due to Marshall Plan incentives."

**Revised (accurate):** "Keynes's International Clearing Union (bancor) was rejected in preliminary bilateral negotiations and never reached the conference agenda. The April 1944 Joint Statement by Experts already reflected a compromise much closer to White's IMF proposal than to Keynes's ICU. By the time delegates convened at Bretton Woods, the conference agenda focused exclusively on finalizing the IMF and World Bank agreements, with Harry Dexter White chairing Commission I (The Fund) and Keynes relegated to Commission II (The Bank). Smaller nations supported the American-dominated agreements due to anticipated dollar shortages and hopes for access to American credit and post-war reconstruction assistance."

### Draft Footnote (Corrected Version):

<sup>1</sup> Keynes's International Clearing Union proposal, featuring the bancor as a supranational reserve currency, was effectively rejected before the Bretton Woods Conference convened. The April 21, 1944 "Joint Statement by Experts on the Establishment of an International Monetary Fund" already reflected a compromise much closer to Harry Dexter White's IMF proposal. The May 25, 1944 U.S. invitation specified the conference was "for the purpose of formulating definite proposals for an International Monetary Fund," with no mention of the ICU. Smaller nations supported the American-dominated agreements primarily due to anticipated dollar shortages and the need for access to American credit and post-war reconstruction assistance. Benn Steil, *The Battle of Bretton Woods: John Maynard Keynes, Harry Dexter White, and the Making of a New World Order* (Princeton, NJ: Princeton University Press, 2013); Federal Reserve History, "Creation of the Bretton Woods System," accessed November 7, 2025, https://www.federalreservehistory.org/essays/bretton-woods-created.
