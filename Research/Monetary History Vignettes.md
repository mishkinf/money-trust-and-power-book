## VIGNETTE 1: British Coal Miner - GOLD STANDARD 1920s [HIGHEST PRIORITY]

### Complete Biographical Details:

- **Location:** 21 Rosebery Terrace, Station Road, Boldon Colliery, County Durham

### Employment Trajectory - Clear Causation Chain:
- **June 1921:** Unemployed (documented in 1921 Census)
- **1925-1926:** Employed at Boldon Colliery when Churchill announced gold standard (April 28, 1925)
- **April 30, 1926:** Locked out when mine owners demanded wage cuts resulting from overvalued pound
- **May 4-12, 1926:** Participated in 9-day General Strike
- **April 1926-November 1926:** 17-month miners' strike at Boldon
- **November 1926:** Forced back to work at reduced wages
- **1930s:** Periodic unemployment; occasional work at Boldon Brickworks

### Wages Documentation:
- **1920 (national average):** 15 shillings 1½ pence per shift
- **1924:** 10 shillings 3¼ pence per shift (already declining before gold standard)
- **Post-strike 1926:** 7 shillings (35 pence) per day - devastating cut

## VIGNETTE 2: American Farm Family - DEPRESSION DEFLATION 1930s

### Status: ✓ STRONG - Excellent Numbers, Name Anonymized in Source

**Individual Identified:** "Farmer A" from William G. Murray study (Iowa State University agricultural economist)

### Transaction Details - Exceptionally Precise:

**Original Farm Holdings (1919):**
- **Land:** 311 acres owned since at least 1909
- **Debt:** $11,000 mortgage ($35/acre)
- **Status:** Conservative, established farmer

**Peak Boom Purchase (March 1920):**
- **New land:** 240 acres purchased for $95,000 ($396/acre - at market peak)
- **Down payment:** $50,000 cash (over 50% - very conservative)
- **Financing:** $45,000 mortgage on new farm
- **Additional borrowing:** Increased home farm mortgage from $11,000 to $45,000 (borrowed additional $34,000)
- **Working capital:** Used $16,000 from deposits and bank loans for livestock/equipment
- **Total debt:** $90,000 on 551 acres ($163/acre average)
- **Debt-to-value ratio:** 40% (considered conservative)

### The Deflation Mechanism - Crystal Clear:

**Corn Prices:**
- **Summer 1919:** $2.00/bushel
- **December 1921:** $0.41/bushel (79.5% decline in 18 months)
- **1921-1925 average:** $0.63/bushel
- **Never recovered** to $1.00+ during 1920s

**The Math of Debt Deflation:**
- When Farmer A borrowed $90,000 in March 1920, corn was $2.00/bushel
- He calculated repaying debt with revenue from approximately 45,000 bushels over time
- By 1921, corn at $0.41/bushel meant **the same $90,000 debt required selling 219,512 bushels**
- **Real debt burden increased 5x** in commodity terms
- Annual interest payment: $5,000 (approximately 5.5% rate)
- This **exceeded the entire value** of corn produced on both farms in 1921

### Crisis Progression (1921-1928):
- **1921:** Initial shock from corn price collapse
- **1921-1927:** Borrowed from "banks, relatives, any other sources available" to make interest payments
- **Each year:** Hoped for corn prices above $1.00/bushel - "that never happened"
- **1927-1928:** Lost both farms through foreclosure after exhausting all borrowing capacity

### Outcome:
Total loss of both farms despite:
- Paying over 50% cash down payment
- Being "conservative" in debt-to-value ratio
- Working diligently to service debt for 6-7 years
- Borrowing from every available source

### Documentation Quality: HIGH CONFIDENCE (except name)

**Primary Source:**
- William G. Murray, "Iowa Land Values: 1803-1967," *The Palimpsest* (State Historical Society of Iowa, October 1967)
- Based on courthouse records Murray examined as Iowa State agricultural economist
- Republished and analyzed by Iowa State University scholars (Zhang & Tidgren, 2018)

**Why Name Is Anonymized:**
Murray used courthouse foreclosure records but anonymized farmers in published work (standard practice for 1960s research ethics). The transaction details, amounts, dates, and outcome are documented from legal records.

**Strengths:**
- Exceptionally precise financial data (amounts, dates, prices)
- Clear demonstration of deflation mechanism
- Well-documented in scholarly publication
- Corroborated by extensive aggregate data on farm foreclosures
- Murray was leading agricultural economist; work is authoritative

**Methodology Note Required:**
"This narrative is based on a specific documented case from Iowa courthouse records analyzed by Iowa State University agricultural economist William G. Murray. The farmer's name was anonymized in Murray's published work, but all financial details (debt amounts, land prices, corn prices, timeline, and foreclosure outcome) are documented from legal records."

### Supplementary Material Found:

**Oscar Heline** (Marcus, Iowa) - interviewed by Studs Terkel in *Hard Times*:
- Named individual, firsthand witness to 1920s farm crisis
- Lived through both 1920s and 1930s agricultural deflation
- Can provide authentic voice/quotes about the era

- Could supplement narrative with direct testimony

**Aggregate Context:**
- Farm mortgage debt tripled 1910-1920 (from $3.2B to $9.6B nationally)
- Foreclosure rate: 3.2 per 1,000 farms (1913-1920) → 17.4 per 1,000 (1926) → 38.8 per 1,000 (1933)
- Over 200,000 farms foreclosed in 1933 alone

### Why This Works Well:

The specific numbers ($90,000 debt, $2.00 vs. $0.41 corn prices, 5x real debt burden increase) make abstract deflation theory viscerally clear. Combined with transparency about methodology, this meets book requirements.

## VIGNETTE 4: Medieval Tally Stick User

### Status: ✓ EXCELLENT - Rare Physical Evidence Surviving

**Individual Identified:** Fulk (Fulco) Basset, Bishop of London

### Biographical Details - Well-Documented:
- **Full name:** Fulk Basset
- **Birth:** c. 1190
- **Death:** 21 May 1259 (buried 25 May 1259) - died of plague sweeping London
- **Family:** Younger son of Alan Basset, baron of Wycombe, Buckinghamshire
- **Brother:** Gilbert Basset (died 1241 in riding accident), Loftus (also mentioned)

### Career Trajectory:
- **October 1239:** Dean of York (prestigious northern ecclesiastical position)
- **December 1241:** Elected Bishop of London by St. Paul's Cathedral chapter
- **October 9, 1244:** Finally consecrated (3-year delay for papal confirmation)
- **1258:** Served on commission for Provisions of Oxford reform movement
- **1259:** Died in office, victim of plague

### The Transaction - Physically Documented:

**Tally Stick Details:**
- **Amount:** £9 4s 4d (nine pounds, four shillings, four pence = 2,256 silver pennies)
- **Purpose:** "for the farm of Wycombe"
- **"Farm":** Fixed annual rent (from Latin *firma*) - payment for right to collect revenues from episcopal or family estates in Wycombe area
- **Date:** Third quarter 13th century (c. 1250-1259, before Fulk's death)
- **Creditor:** King Henry III of England
- **Physical evidence:** Actual tally stick survives - hickory wood, c. 175-200mm (7-8 inches) long, notched and inscribed on both sides
- **Current status:** Survived 1834 Westminster fire when obsolete tallies were being burned; now in private collection

### How the Tally Stick Worked - Documented Procedure:

**From *Dialogus de Scaccario* (c. 1179) by Richard Fitznigel:**

1. **Notching system (standardized):**
   - Thickness of palm: £1,000
   - Breadth of thumb: £100
   - Breadth of little finger: £20
   - Width of swollen barleycorn: £1
   - Narrower grooves: shillings
   - Single cut without removing wood: pence

2. **Inscription:** Clerk wrote transaction details in ink on both sides: "£9.4s.4d. From Fulk Basset for the farm of Wycombe"

3. **The Split:** Stick split lengthwise from one end
   - Created two pieces with identical notches and inscriptions
   - Unique grain patterns matched like fingerprints - virtually forgery-proof
   - **Stock** (longer piece): Given to payer (Fulk) as receipt
   - **Foil** (shorter piece): Kept in Exchequer treasury

4. **Verification:** Both halves could be matched together to verify authenticity

### Social Context of Fulk's Life:

**As Bishop of London:**
- Great feudal magnate controlling vast estates
- Presiding over ecclesiastical courts
- Sitting on king's council
- Attending Parliament as one of "Lords Spiritual"
- Managing complex web of financial obligations to Crown
- Spiritual duties: ordaining priests, consecrating churches, diocesan administration
- Temporal duties: estate management, rent collection, Crown negotiations

**Payment Context:**
£9 4s 4d represented approximately:
- Annual income from several manors
- Cost of maintaining a knight for a year
- Substantial but not ruinous for a wealthy bishop

**Exchequer Visit:**
When Fulk made payment, he witnessed elaborate ritual:
- Entered the Receipt at Westminster Palace
- Tellers counted 2,256 individual silver pennies
- Coins checked for weight and purity on checkered cloth (gave Exchequer its name)
- Tally cut and ceremonially divided
- Fulk departed with wooden stock as receipt

### Tally Sticks as Negotiable Instruments:
By 13th century, Exchequer tallies had become tradable in secondary markets:
- Could be traded at discount before redemption
- A creditor of the bishop might accept tally as payment
- Could later be presented to tax collectors as proof of payment owed
- Functioned as early form of credit money

### Documentation Quality: HIGH CONFIDENCE

**Primary Sources:**
- The physical tally stick itself (extraordinary survival)
- *Dialogus de Scaccario* (c. 1179) - contemporary Exchequer manual
- Dictionary of National Biography (1885) - Fulk Basset biography
- Multiple medieval records listing Fulk's positions and death

**Secondary Sources:**
- Hilary Jenkinson, "Exchequer Tallies" (1911) and "Medieval Tallies" (1924) in *Archaeologia*
- M.T. Clanchy, *From Memory to Written Record* (1979)
- Richard Cassidy's recent work on Pipe Rolls and Exchequer
- Tanja Skambraks on tallies as media of knowledge

**Methodology Note:**
Narrative based on:
- **DOCUMENTED:** Fulk's biographical facts, exact tally transaction, Exchequer procedures from contemporary sources, physical tally survival
- **CONTEXTUAL INFERENCE:** Daily episcopal life details, Exchequer visit narrative, typical transactions - historically accurate reconstructions from scholarly sources

### Why This Works Exceptionally Well:

**Physical Survival:** Having actual tally stick survive 750+ years (including 1834 fire) is extraordinary. This is tangible medieval money.

**High-Status Subject:** Bishop provides window into medieval financial administration at highest levels, yet tally stick system was universal (used by sheriffs, merchants, manorial courts).

**Perfect Demonstration:** Shows how credit/money worked without printing, banks, or literacy - solved fundamental trust problem through ingenious wood-splitting technology.

**Modern Parallels:** Medieval "blockchain" - distributed ledger, tamper-proof, trustless verification. Demonstrates money as credit instrument predates modern banking by centuries.

**System Longevity:** Tally sticks used from Norman times until 1826 - 700-year technology. Their obsolescence triggered 1834 Parliament fire when thousands were burned.

## Research Methodology Notes

### What Worked Well:

1. **Secondary sources citing primary sources:** Most efficient path - historians who've already done archival work often cite specific cases
2. **Family archives and oral histories:** Harry Todd case came from family preserving history
3. **Academic agricultural economists:** Murray's work provided precise farm debt data
4. **Recent journalism for recent events:** PBS NewsHour excellent for 2008 case
5. **Physical evidence:** Tally stick survival enabled Fulk Basset case

### Challenges Encountered:

1. **Privacy protections:** Modern social science research uses anonymization/pseudonyms
2. **Digitization gaps:** 1970s union publications, some oral histories not fully online
3. **Documentary survival:** Medieval full biographies rare; outcomes often unknown
4. **Longitudinal data:** Hard to find families tracked across multiple years with wage/price data
5. **Congressional testimony access:** Hearings exist but detailed transcripts not always digitized/searchable at individual name level

### Source Quality Hierarchy Observed:

**Highest Confidence:**
- Official government records (census, Pipe Rolls, BLS/Census statistics)
- Physical evidence (tally stick)
- Contemporary primary sources (*Dialogus de Scaccario*, newspapers from era)
- Academic work based on courthouse/archive research

**Medium-High Confidence:**
- Reputable journalism (PBS, major newspapers)
- Family oral histories (subject to memory but often detailed)
- Secondary scholarship citing primary sources

**Medium Confidence:**
- Press releases (verifiable but promotional)
- Anonymized case studies (authentic but identity protected)

**Required Caution:**
- Single-source stories (seek corroboration)
- Memories recorded decades after events (valuable but verify when possible)
- Composite/representative cases (must be transparent about methodology)
