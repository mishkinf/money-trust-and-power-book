# Money as Moral Philosophy: 4,000 Years of Economic Justice Arguments

## Bottom Line Up Front

**Economics has always been moral philosophy making normative choices about whose interests matter**—until recently when it pretended otherwise. From ancient Mesopotamian debt jubilees to Aristotle's condemnation of usury, from Adam Smith's progressive taxation principles to John Maynard Keynes exposing how monetary policy deliberately creates unemployment, history reveals a sophisticated tradition of explicit moral-economic reasoning that modern "neutral" economics obscures. This research documents how every generation faced the same fundamental questions about wealth distribution, taxation justice, and economic rights—questions resolved through moral argument, not technical necessity. Today's policy debates about student debt cancellation, billionaire wealth taxes, and climate finance replay ancient philosophical disputes with new vocabulary, while mounting behavioral and cross-cultural evidence confirms: **extreme inequality violates universal human fairness intuitions, wealth concentration threatens democracy, and financial constraints are political choices, not economic laws**.

The evidence is overwhelming: 2.9 million Americans work full-time yet remain poor, essential workers earn poverty wages despite society's dependence on them, and asset prices have grown four times faster than wages since 1960. Meanwhile, cross-cultural research from Islamic zakat to indigenous gift economies to Ubuntu philosophy shows wealth circulation and communal benefit consistently prioritized over individual accumulation across human societies. Modern behavioral studies confirm this isn't cultural preference but reflects deep psychological fairness intuitions—ultimatum game research across 15 societies found zero cultures exhibiting purely self-interested behavior. This matters profoundly: when political leaders claim "we can't afford" healthcare or climate action, they're making moral choices about priorities while pretending financial necessity. History proves democratic governments can finance any priority within productive capacity—WWII mobilization increased federal spending from 1.4% to 37% of GDP in five years. The constraints are political will, not money.

## I. ANCIENT & MEDIEVAL: The Moral Foundations

### Debt Jubilees: The 4,000-Year Precedent

Long before modern economics pretended to separate moral from technical questions, ancient civilizations recognized excessive debt concentration as existential threat requiring periodic cancellation. Mesopotamian kings proclaimed "Clean Slate" edicts (andurarum, amargi, misharum) to restore economic balance, liberate debt bondservants, and maintain military-capable populations. These weren't acts of charity but **pragmatic economic stabilization preventing oligarchic takeover and social collapse**.

The sophistication is striking: **Hammurabi canceled debts four times during his reign** (1792, 1780, 1771, 1762 BCE), demonstrating institutionalized recognition that debt accumulation follows predictable patterns requiring intervention. Egyptian Pharaoh Bakenranef abolished debt bondage with explicit reasoning: soldier-citizens' bodies "should belong to the state" for military service—"it would be absurd for a soldier...to be haled to prison by his creditor for an unpaid loan" while enemies attack.

**Citations:**
- Hudson, Michael. *...and forgive them their debts: Lending, Foreclosure and Redemption from Bronze Age Finance to the Jubilee Year*. Dresden: ISLET-Verlag, 2018. [PRINT]
- Toussaint, Éric. "The Long Tradition of Debt Cancellation in Mesopotamia and Egypt." CADTM. https://www.cadtm.org/The-Long-Tradition-of-Debt [OPEN ACCESS]

Biblical traditions codified this wisdom into religious law. Deuteronomy 15 mandates seven-year debt release: "At the end of every seven years you must grant a release...every creditor shall release what he has lent to his neighbor." Leviticus 25 establishes the Jubilee: "You shall hallow the fiftieth year and you shall proclaim liberty throughout the land to all its inhabitants...you shall return every one to his property." The moral reasoning is explicit: "The land shall not be sold in perpetuity, for **the land is mine**; with me you are but aliens and tenants."

**Citation:**
- *The Holy Bible: New Revised Standard Version*. Nashville: Thomas Nelson Publishers, 1989. [PRINT/LIBRARY]

**Contemporary Connection:** This connects directly to **modern student debt cancellation debates**. When critics claim debt cancellation is unprecedented radicalism, history provides 4,000 years of counterevidence showing debt relief as recurring necessity for economic stability.

### Aristotle: Natural vs. Unnatural Wealth

Aristotle (384-322 BCE) distinguished between **oikonomia** (household management serving community needs) and **chrematistics** (wealth accumulation as end in itself). His condemnation of usury remains strikingly relevant: "**The most hated sort [of wealth-getting], and with the greatest reason, is usury, which makes a gain out of money itself, and not from the natural object of it**. For money was intended to be used in exchange, but not to increase at interest...Wherefore of all modes of getting wealth this is the most unnatural."

**Citation:**
- Aristotle. *Politics*. Translated by Benjamin Jowett. Oxford: Clarendon Press, 1885. Book I, Chapter 10, 1258b1-8. [PRINT/LIBRARY]
- Perseus Digital Library: http://www.perseus.tufts.edu/hopper/text?doc=Perseus:text:1999.01.0058 [OPEN ACCESS]

The moral logic: money is technology for facilitating exchange, not commodity that "breeds" more money. When interest-bearing debt becomes primary economic activity, society prioritizes extraction over production.

### Aquinas: Common Use and Just Price

Thomas Aquinas (1225-1274) embedded economic morality into Christian theology. On usury: "To take usury for money lent is unjust in itself, because this is to sell what does not exist, and this evidently leads to inequality which is contrary to justice." On property: "Man ought to possess external things, not as his own, but as common, so that, to wit, **he is ready to communicate them to others in their need**."

**Citation:**
- Aquinas, Thomas. *Summa Theologiae*. Translated by Fathers of the English Dominican Province. London: Burns, Oates & Washbourne, 1920. II-II, Q.78, Art.1; Q.66, Art.2. [PRINT]
- New Advent (online): https://www.newadvent.org/summa/3078.htm [OPEN ACCESS]

This "common use principle" establishes private property as conditional, not absolute. Wealth accumulation carries obligation to serve common good.

### Ibn Khaldun: The Original Laffer Curve

Ibn Khaldun (1332-1406) developed sophisticated taxation theory: "At the beginning of the dynasty, taxation yields a large revenue from small assessments. At the end of the dynasty, taxation yields a small revenue from large assessments."

**Citation:**
- Ibn Khaldun. *The Muqaddimah: An Introduction to History*. Translated by Franz Rosenthal. 3 vols. 2nd ed. Princeton: Princeton University Press, 1967. 2:89-92. [LIBRARY/PRINT]

## II. EARLY MODERN: Political Economy as Moral Philosophy

### Adam Smith: The Misunderstood Moral Philosopher

Adam Smith held the Chair of Moral Philosophy at Glasgow, viewing economics as inseparable from ethics. **Smith's Four Maxims of Taxation**:

**Maxim I (Ability to Pay)**: "The subjects of every state ought to contribute towards the support of the government, as nearly as possible, **in proportion to their respective abilities**."

**On Public Infrastructure**: "The duty of erecting and maintaining certain publick works...which it can never be for the interest of any individual, or small number of individuals, to erect and maintain; because the profit could never repay the expence to any individual...though it may frequently do much more than repay it to a great society."

**Smith on Rent-Seeking**: "As soon as the land of any country has all become private property, the landlords, like all other men, **love to reap where they never sowed, and demand a rent even for its natural produce**."

**Citations:**
- Smith, Adam. *An Inquiry into the Nature and Causes of the Wealth of Nations*. Edited by Edwin Cannan. 5th ed. London: Methuen & Co., 1904. Reprint, Library of Economics and Liberty, 2000. https://www.econlib.org/library/Smith/smWN.html [OPEN ACCESS]

### Thomas Paine: The Citizen's Dividend

*Agrarian Justice* (1797) presents radical argument: "**Men did not make the earth**...Every proprietor, therefore, of cultivated lands, owes to the community a ground-rent (for I know of no better term to express the idea) for the land which he holds."

Paine proposed taxing land inheritance to fund **universal payments of £15 at age 21 and £10 annually after age 50**. His moral reasoning: "In advocating the case of the persons thus dispossessed, **it is a right, and not a charity, that I am pleading for**."

**Citations:**
- Paine, Thomas. *Agrarian Justice*. 1797. In *Thomas Paine: Collected Writings*, edited by Eric Foner, 396-413. New York: Library of America, 1995. [LIBRARY]
- Also available at https://www.ushistory.org/paine/agrarian/ [OPEN ACCESS]

**Modern parallel**: The Alaska Permanent Fund distributes oil revenue dividends, proving Paine's concept viable.

## III. NINETEENTH CENTURY: Earned vs. Unearned Income

### John Stuart Mill: The Sleep Quote

Mill (1806-1873) provided the clearest moral distinction between earned and unearned income:

"The ordinary progress of a society which increases in wealth, is at all times tending to augment the incomes of landlords...**independently of any trouble or outlay incurred by themselves. They grow richer, as it were in their sleep, without working, risking, or economizing**. What claim have they, on the general principle of social justice, to this accession of riches?"

His policy prescription: "**It is not the fortunes which are earned, but those which are unearned, that it is for the public good to place under limitation**."

**Citations:**
- Mill, John Stuart. *Principles of Political Economy with Some of Their Applications to Social Philosophy*. 7th ed. Edited by William J. Ashley. London: Longmans, Green and Co., 1909. Book V, Chapter II, Sections 3-5. Available at Library of Economics and Liberty. https://www.econlib.org/library/Mill/mlP.html [OPEN ACCESS]

### Henry George: Progress and Poverty

George (1839-1897) asked: why does poverty deepen alongside technological progress? His answer: landowners capture socially-created value.

"**The tax upon land values is, therefore, the most just and equal of all taxes. It falls only upon those who receive from society a peculiar and valuable benefit**, and upon them in proportion to the benefit they receive. It is the taking by the community, for the use of the community, of that value which is the creation of the community."

**Citations:**
- George, Henry. *Progress and Poverty: An Inquiry into the Cause of Industrial Depressions and of Increase of Want with Increase of Wealth: The Remedy*. New York: D. Appleton and Company, 1879. Available at https://henrygeorge.org/pcontents.htm [OPEN ACCESS]

**Contemporary data**: Housing prices increased **121% (inflation-adjusted) from 1960-2024 while median household income rose only 29%**.

### David Ricardo: Rent as Monopoly Price

Ricardo (1772-1823) demonstrated that landlords capture differential value without contributing to production: "Rent is that portion of the produce of the earth which is paid to the landlord for the use of the **original and indestructible powers of the soil**."

**Citations:**
- Ricardo, David. *On the Principles of Political Economy and Taxation*. 3rd ed. London: John Murray, 1821. Chapter II. Available at Library of Economics and Liberty. https://www.econlib.org/library/Ricardo/ricP.html [OPEN ACCESS]

## IV. TWENTIETH CENTURY: Exposing Technical Choices as Moral Decisions

### Keynes: Deflation as Class Warfare

Keynes (1883-1946) exposed "technical" monetary policy as disguised moral-political choice. His 1925 essay "The Economic Consequences of Mr. Churchill" remains devastating critique.

"**Deflation does not reduce wages 'automatically.' It reduces them by causing unemployment**."

"When we raise the value of sterling by 10 per cent we **transfer about £1,000,000,000 into the pockets of the rentiers out of the pockets of the rest of us**."

The Bank of England's credit restriction "**deliberately intensifies the unemployment**" to achieve wage deflation.

**Citations:**
- Keynes, John Maynard. "The Economic Consequences of Mr. Churchill." In *Essays in Persuasion*. London: Hogarth Press, 1925. Reprinted in *The Collected Writings of John Maynard Keynes*, vol. 9, 207-230. London: Macmillan for the Royal Economic Society, 1972. [LIBRARY]

**Modern parallel**: Federal Reserve rate hikes follow identical logic—**deliberately creating unemployment to reduce wage pressures**.

### John Rawls: The Difference Principle

Rawls (1921-2002) asked: what principles of justice would we choose behind a "veil of ignorance"? His answer: "Social and economic inequalities...are to be to the **greatest benefit of the least-advantaged members of society** (the difference principle)."

**Citations:**
- Rawls, John. *A Theory of Justice*. Revised edition. Cambridge, MA: Harvard University Press, 1999. [Original 1971] [LIBRARY]
- Rawls, John. *Justice as Fairness: A Restatement*. Edited by Erin Kelly. Cambridge, MA: Harvard University Press, 2001. [LIBRARY]

### Amartya Sen & Martha Nussbaum: Capabilities Approach

Sen demonstrated that **equality of opportunity is insufficient without equality of capabilities**—what people can actually do and be. Nussbaum specified **ten central capabilities** every society must secure.

**Citations:**
- Sen, Amartya. *Development as Freedom*. New York: Oxford University Press, 1999. [LIBRARY]
- Nussbaum, Martha C. *Women and Human Development: The Capabilities Approach*. Cambridge: Cambridge University Press, 2000. [LIBRARY]

## VIII. BEHAVIORAL EVIDENCE: Wealth and Fairness

### Paul Piff: Wealth Reduces Empathy

UC Berkeley research: Households earning $50,000-$75,000 gave **7.6% of income to charity**; those making $100,000+ gave only **4.2%**.

"As a person's levels of wealth increase, **their feelings of compassion and empathy go down, and their feelings of entitlement increases**."

**Good news**: Simply showing 46-second video on childhood poverty made wealthy subjects "just as generous...as someone who's poor." Wealth's effect on empathy is reversible.

**Citations:**
- Piff, Paul K., et al. "Awe, the Small Self, and Prosocial Behavior." *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology* 108, no. 6 (2015): 883-899. https://doi.org/10.1037/pspi0000018 [LIBRARY]

### Melvin Lerner: Just World Hypothesis

Lerner's experiments demonstrated humans' tendency to believe "people get what they deserve." Observers watching random electric shocks **"began to reject and devalue the victim"**, persuading themselves the victim deserved suffering.

**Citations:**
- Lerner, Melvin J. *The Belief in a Just World: A Fundamental Delusion*. New York: Plenum Press, 1980. https://doi.org/10.1007/978-1-4899-0448-5 [LIBRARY]

### Ultimatum Game: Universal Fairness

Across 15 small-scale societies, researchers found **no society showed purely self-interested behavior**. Modal offer: 40-50% of total; offers below 20% rejected ~50% of time despite responders sacrificing money to punish unfairness.

**Citations:**
- Henrich, Joseph, et al. *Foundations of Human Sociality: Economic Experiments and Ethnographic Evidence from Fifteen Small-Scale Societies*. Oxford: Oxford University Press, 2004. [LIBRARY]

### Easterlin Paradox: Money's Diminishing Returns

"At a point in time happiness varies directly with income, but over time **happiness does not trend upward as income continues to grow**."

Kahneman & Deaton: **Emotional well-being plateaus around $75,000/year**.

**Citations:**
- Easterlin, Richard A. "Does Economic Growth Improve the Human Lot?" In *Nations and Households in Economic Growth*, edited by Paul A. David and Melvin W. Reder, 89-125. New York: Academic Press, 1974. [LIBRARY]
- Kahneman, Daniel, and Angus Deaton. "High Income Improves Evaluation of Life but Not Emotional Well-Being." *PNAS* 107, no. 38 (2010): 16489-16493. https://doi.org/10.1073/pnas.1011492107 [OPEN ACCESS]

## XI. MYTH-BUSTING: Eight Prevalent Beliefs Demolished

### MYTH 1: "They earned every penny through hard work"

**Five Counterarguments**:
1. **Inheritance Reality**: 33% of billionaires inherited wealth entirely
2. **Paine**: "Men did not make the earth"—wealth relies on social infrastructure
3. **George**: Wealth from land value created by community development
4. **Piketty**: r>g—wealth inequality grows through mathematical dynamic, not merit
5. **Stiglitz**: Much top income from rent-seeking, not productivity

**Key fact**: 80% of "self-made" billionaires came from upper-middle-class or wealthy backgrounds.

### MYTH 2: "Taxing the rich stops innovation/investment"

**Five Counterarguments**:
1. **1950s**: Top rate 91-94% with 4.2% GDP growth—fastest in modern history
2. **LSE study**: 50-year analysis shows tax cuts for rich had "**zero**" effect on GDP/employment
3. **Intrinsic motivation**: Scientists driven by problem-solving, not after-tax income
4. **TCJA failure**: 2017 cuts produced stock buybacks, not investment
5. **Comparative evidence**: No correlation between top tax rates and innovation metrics

**Key quote**: "No evidence that top tax rates affect economic growth" (Congressional Research Service, 1945-2010).

### MYTH 3: "The rich create jobs"

**Five Counterarguments**:
1. **Keynesian economics**: Consumer demand creates jobs, not wealthy investors
2. **Trickle-down failure**: Reagan to Trump—all promised job creation, all failed
3. **Rent-seeking**: Tax savings flow to stock buybacks and executive pay
4. **Public investment multiplier**: Government spending has higher employment effect
5. **Alaska Permanent Fund**: Direct payments showed no negative employment effects

**Key fact**: Job growth stronger after 1993 and 2013 top-rate increases than after 2001/2003 cuts.

### MYTH 4: "Government debt burdens future generations"

**Five Counterarguments**:
1. **Sovereign currency**: Countries issuing own currency cannot involuntarily default
2. **WWII debt reduction**: US reduced debt-to-GDP from 106% to 23% (1946-1974) through growth, not austerity
3. **Debt vs investment**: Debt financing productive investment benefits future generations
4. **Private debt danger**: Private debt causes crises (2008), not public debt
5. **Intergenerational transfer**: Future generations inherit both assets (infrastructure) and liabilities (bonds)

**Key fact**: Japanese debt-to-GDP over 250% without crisis.

### MYTH 5: "We can't afford [policy X] without raising taxes first"

**Five Counterarguments**:
1. **WWII financing**: US raised deficit to 27.5% of GDP before "affording" it
2. **Real constraints**: Real constraint is productive capacity, not money
3. **Keynes on liquidity trap**: Government spending costless in real terms during recessions

5. **Opportunity cost**: If resources unemployed, opportunity cost is zero

**Key fact**: COVID response—spent trillions without "affording" it first. Fed balance sheet: $4T to $9T (2019-2022) without hyperinflation.

### MYTH 6: "Poverty exists because poor don't work hard enough"

**Five Counterarguments**:

## CONCLUSION: Reclaiming Economic Justice as Moral Philosophy

This research establishes five incontrovertible conclusions:

**First**: Economic arrangements have always embodied moral choices about whose interests matter. From Mesopotamian debt jubilees to Aristotle's usury condemnation, from Adam Smith's ability-to-pay principle to Keynes exposing deflation as class warfare, every generation explicitly debated distribution justice. Modern claims that economics is "neutral science" represent historical amnesia, not progress.

**Second**: Contemporary policy debates replay ancient moral disputes. Student debt cancellation echoes 4,000 years of jubilee traditions. Billionaire wealth taxes resurrect Mill's earned-versus-unearned distinction. Climate finance mirrors WWII mobilization proving financial constraints are political choices. Housing speculation confirms George's warnings about land value capture. These aren't new questions—they're eternal tensions about power, justice, and obligation that each generation must resolve.

**Third**: Cross-cultural and behavioral evidence demolishes "inequality is natural" mythology. Islamic zakat, Ubuntu philosophy, indigenous gift economies, and Confucian ethics all prioritize wealth circulation over accumulation. Ultimatum game research across 15 societies found universal fairness preferences—humans aren't naturally self-interested maximizers. Piff's studies show wealth temporarily reduces empathy but interventions easily reverse this. The psychological research is clear: extreme inequality violates innate human fairness intuitions that predate capitalism.

**Fourth**: Structural mechanisms reveal how money distributes power. The Cantillon effect shows QE enriched asset owners while wages stagnated. Asset price inflation (housing +121%, stocks +3,100% since 1970) versus wage growth (+29%, +500%) concentrates wealth among existing owners. The FIRE sector captures 25-30% of corporate profits while producing 7-8% of GDP—textbook rent extraction. These aren't market outcomes but design choices embedded in monetary architecture.

**Fifth**: The working poor prove poverty isn't about effort. 2.9 million full-time workers remain poor despite employment. Essential workers earn poverty wages ($15.18/hour for home health aides) despite society's dependence on them. The "boots theory" reveals being poor is expensive—lack of capital access traps people in costly cycles. Thompson and Polanyi documented how capitalism actively created poverty by transforming independent producers into wage laborers. Structural barriers, not individual failings, perpetuate deprivation.

The synthesis is inescapable: **money is political technology whose design determines wealth distribution, not neutral medium of exchange**. Every monetary arrangement—how money is created, who receives it first, what activities it rewards—embodies moral commitments about whose flourishing matters. When central banks "independently" set interest rates, when governments claim they "can't afford" social programs, when economists declare inequality "efficient"—these are political exercises of power dressed as technical necessity.

The stakes are existential. As Brandeis warned: "We can have democracy, or we can have great wealth concentrated in the hands of a few, but we can't have both." Piketty's r>g dynamic creates hereditary aristocracy incompatible with meritocracy. Deaton's deaths of despair—158,000 annually—reveal inequality's human costs. Behavioral research confirms wealth corrupts empathy while poverty is psychologically devastating. The climate crisis demands mobilization comparable to WWII, yet "we can't afford it" rhetoric paralyzes action while financial constraints remain political choices, not economic laws.

Democratic renewal requires reclaiming this historical memory. The social contract tradition from Hobbes through Rawls establishes that government must protect citizens from economic violence, provide essential common goods, and ensure broad prosperity. Historical precedent proves societies can afford what they can physically accomplish—WWII mobilization increased federal spending from 1.4% to 37% of GDP in five years. Real constraints are productive capacity and political will, not money. When Alaska distributes oil revenue as citizen dividends, when Iceland prosecutes bankers after 2008, when post-WWII Germany received comprehensive debt relief—these demonstrate alternatives exist.

The verdict of history is clear: every economic system makes moral choices about distribution. The question isn't whether to make these choices but whose interests prevail. For four millennia, societies recognized debt accumulation requires periodic relief, wealth concentration threatens stability, and extreme inequality violates justice. Modern "neutrality" claims don't eliminate these moral dimensions—they obscure whose interests benefit from current arrangements while pretending inevitability.
