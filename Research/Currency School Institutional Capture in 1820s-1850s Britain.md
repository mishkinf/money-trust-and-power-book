# Research Report: Currency School Institutional Capture in 1820s-1850s Britain

## Critical finding requiring thesis revision

This research has uncovered a fundamental contradiction to the chapter's premise regarding The Economist: **James Wilson and The Economist were Banking School advocates who opposed the Currency School and the Bank Charter Act 1844**. This finding, however, reveals where Currency School institutional capture actually succeeded—through parliamentary legislation and potentially academic channels, rather than through the commercial press.

## TIER 1 FINDINGS

### 1. James Wilson and The Economist (1843): A Banking School Publication

#### Biographical Profile

**James Wilson** (June 3, 1805, Hawick, Scotland – August 11, 1860, Calcutta, India) was the fourth of fifteen children born to William Wilson, a Quaker woollen manufacturer. After apprenticing to a hat manufacturer at age sixteen, Wilson's father purchased a hat business for him and his brother around 1824. The firm Wilson, Irwin & Wilson operated in London until its dissolution in 1831, after which Wilson continued as James Wilson & Co. with considerable success, accumulating a net worth of £25,000 by 1837 (equivalent to approximately £2,869,419 in 2023 values).

The Panic of 1837 severely damaged Wilson's business interests, prompting a career pivot toward political economy writing. Between 1839 and 1843, he published three influential pamphlets attacking the Corn Laws, most notably *Influences of the Corn Laws, as affecting all classes of the community, and particularly the landed interests* (London: Longman, Orme, Brown, Green, and Longmans, 1840), which became known as "the most sophisticated analysis of the effects of the monopoly of the corn laws by a member of the League." Wilson collaborated closely with Richard Cobden and Charles Villiers, principal spokesmen for the Anti-Corn Law League, consulting with them before establishing The Economist.

**Citation:** "Wilson, James (1805-1860)," in *Dictionary of National Biography, 1885-1900*, ed. Sidney Lee, vol. 62 (London: Smith, Elder & Co., 1900); Asa Briggs, "Wilson, James (1805–1860)," in *The New Palgrave Dictionary of Economics*, ed. Macmillan Publishers (London: Palgrave Macmillan, 2018).

#### The Economist Founding and Financial Backing

The first issue of *The Economist: The Political, Commercial, Agricultural, and Free-Trade Journal* appeared on September 2, 1843, following a preliminary prospectus issue in August 1843. Wilson served as chief editor and sole proprietor, writing "nearly the whole of the paper" in its early years. The magazine's stated purpose encompassed thirteen areas of coverage, centered on applying free-trade principles to practical commercial and agricultural topics, with the primary goal of campaigning for repeal of the Corn Laws (achieved in 1846).

Lord Radnor, described as an "ardent free-trader," provided the primary documented financial backing, though the *Dictionary of National Biography* notes that Wilson "invested most of his capital and obtained some help from Lord Radnor." **No specific monetary amounts are documented in available sources.**

The magazine achieved modest but influential circulation: 1,800 copies in 1844, rising to 3,800 by 1850 and 4,300 by 1854. Despite these relatively small numbers, it "at once became a recognised power in the newspaper world" and "quickly became invaluable as a source of trade and financial statistics," targeting "financial and commercial men" and policy-makers as its core readership.

**Citation:** "Wilson, James," *Dictionary of National Biography*; Ruth Dudley Edwards, *The Pursuit of Reason: The Economist 1843-1993* (London: Hamish Hamilton, 1993).

#### The Critical Evidence: Wilson as Banking School Advocate

Multiple authoritative sources establish Wilson's alignment with the Banking School in opposition to Currency School monetary doctrine:

**Academic Classification:** Laurent Le Maux identifies Wilson explicitly as a Banking School contributor alongside Thomas Tooke (1840, 1844, 1848), John Fullarton (1845), John Stuart Mill (1844, 1848), and William Newmarch (1857, 1866).

**Citation:** Laurent Le Maux, "Central Banking and Finance: The Bank of England and the Bank Act of 1844," *Revue Economique* 69, no. 4 (2018): 571-574.

**Opponent Recognition:** Currency School defender Robert Torrens published *The Principles and Practical Operation of Sir Robert Peel's Bill of 1844 Explained, and Defended Against the Objections of Tooke, Fullarton, and Wilson* (London: Longman, Brown, Green and Longmans, 1848), explicitly grouping Wilson with the two leading Banking School theorists as opponents requiring rebuttal.

**Wilson's Published Critique:** Wilson compiled his Economist articles into *Capital, currency, and banking; being a collection of a series of articles published in the 'Economist' in 1845, on the principles of the Bank Act of 1844, and in 1847, on the recent monetarial and commercial crisis; concluding with a plan for a secure and economical currency* (London: The Office of the Economist, 1847), demonstrating sustained criticism of the Currency School's legislative triumph.

#### Institutional Capture Implications

This finding requires fundamental revision of the chapter's argument. The Economist was **not** an instrument of Currency School institutional capture but rather represented **opposition** to that capture. The Banking School—including Wilson, Tooke, and Fullarton—**lost** the legislative battle when Parliament enacted the Bank Charter Act 1844 based on Currency School principles advocated by Samuel Jones Loyd (later Lord Overstone), George Warde Norman, and Robert Torrens, with Prime Minister Sir Robert Peel's support.

The relevant institutional capture question becomes: **Was The Economist captured by Anti-Corn Law League free-trade interests?** (Answer: Yes, explicitly—that was its founding purpose.) The Currency School's institutional triumph occurred through **parliamentary legislation** and potentially through **academic appointments**, not through control of commercial periodicals.

### 2. Oxford and Cambridge Political Economy Curriculum (1820s-1850s)

#### Oxford: The Drummond Professorship

The Drummond Chair of Political Economy, established in 1825 and named for founder Henry Drummond, represented England's first endowed professorship in the discipline. **Nassau William Senior** (1790-1864) served as inaugural Drummond Professor from 1825 to 1830, followed by **Richard Whately** (1787-1863) from 1829 to 1831 (note the succession overlap). Senior returned for a second term from 1847 to 1852.

Senior, educated at Eton and Magdalen College, Oxford (B.A. 1812), was called to the bar in 1819 and elected Fellow of University College, Oxford. During his first Drummond term, he delivered lectures that were published as a series of influential works:

- *An Introductory Lecture on Political Economy* (London: John Murray, 1827)
- *Three Lectures on the Transmission of the Precious Metals from Country to Country, and the Mercantile Theory of Wealth* (1828)
- *Two Lectures on Population* (1829), with correspondence between author and Malthus
- *Three Lectures on the Cost of obtaining Money, and on some Effects of Private and Government Paper Money* (London: John Murray, 1830)
- *Three Lectures on the Rate of Wages* (1830, 2nd ed. 1831)
- *An Outline of the Science of Political Economy* (1836), compiled from his Oxford lectures

Contemporary sources note that Senior "lectured in Oxford to a disappointingly decreasing audience till 1829," suggesting limited student engagement despite the professorship's prestige.

**Citation:** Marian Bowley, *Nassau Senior and Classical Economics* (London: George Allen & Unwin, 1937; reissued Routledge, 2003); Pietro Corsi, "The teaching of Richard Whately," in *Science and Religion: Baden Powell and the Anglican Debate, 1800–1860* (Cambridge: Cambridge University Press, 1988), 85-95.

Richard Whately succeeded Senior as Drummond Professor, delivering his *Introductory Lectures on Political Economy* in Easter Term 1831 (London: B. Fellowes, 1st ed. 1831; 2nd and enlarged ed. 1832). Whately, a Fellow of Oriel College, aimed to refute "the dominant Oxford view that political economy, being concerned with wealth, was materialistic and opposed to Christianity." As part of the "Oriel Noetics"—a reformist clerical group that "constructed a Christian Political Economy by dichotomising knowledge into a theological domain and a scientific domain"—Whately worked to make political economy acceptable to conservative Oxford opinion. His appointment as Archbishop of Dublin in 1831 cut short his professorship.

**Citation:** Murray N. Rothbard, "Catallactics," in Steven N. Durlauf and Lawrence E. Blume, eds., *The New Palgrave Dictionary of Economics* (London: Palgrave Macmillan, 2008).

#### Cambridge: George Pryme and the Moral Sciences Tripos

**George Pryme** (1781-1868) began conducting lectures on political economy at Cambridge in **1816**—"the first teaching of such a topic at any English university"—a full nine years before Oxford established the Drummond Chair. Educated at Trinity College, Cambridge (B.A. 1803, Scholar 1800) and called to the bar in 1806, Pryme conducted his early lectures with the university senate's "sanction" but had to schedule them to avoid interference with college lectures. His courses "were well attended" initially.

On May 27, 1828, the university senate recognized Pryme as Professor of Political Economy by grace, formalizing his twelve years of teaching. He published *A Syllabus of a Course of Lectures on the Principles of Political Economy* in 1816, establishing a curriculum framework. Pryme also served as Whig MP for Cambridge Borough from 1832 to 1841 and functioned as an official examiner for the new Tripos from 1851 to 1860. The professorship was formally established in perpetuity in 1863 following his retirement.

**Citation:** George Pryme, *Autobiographic Recollections of George Pryme, Esq. M.A.*, ed. by his daughter (Cambridge: Deighton, Bell, and Co., 1870); Keith Tribe and Angela Redish, "George Pryme (1781–1868)," in *Handbook of the History of Economic Thought* (Springer, 2013).

The **Moral Sciences Tripos**, established in 1848 following advocacy by Prince Albert (elected Chancellor 1847) and reformers including Henry Philpott (Master of St. Catharine's) and William Whewell (Master of Trinity), incorporated political economy alongside history, jurisprudence, English law, and moral philosophy. However, "during the first nine years only 66 competed for honours in moral [sciences]," and these new triposes "did not qualify those who took them for the B.A. degree until 1860." From 1852, candidates for the ordinary degree were "required to attend professorial lectures for one term and to pass an examination appointed by the professor."

**Citation:** British History Online, "The University of Cambridge: The age of reforms (1800-82)," in *A History of the County of Cambridge and the Isle of Ely: Volume 3, the City and University of Cambridge*, ed. J. P. C. Roach (London: Victoria County History, 1959), 235-265.

#### Textbook Evidence: Mill's Dominance

John Stuart Mill's *Principles of Political Economy* (London: John W. Parker, 1848) achieved immediate and sustained dominance as the political economy textbook. Running through seven editions during Mill's lifetime (final edition: 1871), the *Principles* was "hailed as a classic, far more congenial to the public than earlier economic textbooks" upon publication. Historian Eric Roll notes that "to many generations of students, Mill's Principles was the undisputed bible of economic doctrine." At Oxford, the text "continued to be used until 1919."

Before Mill's *Principles*, Nassau Senior's *An Outline of the Science of Political Economy* (1836), based on his Oxford lectures, exercised wide influence among classical economists. However, **no documentary evidence specifying which textbooks were assigned in which years at Oxford or Cambridge during the 1820s-1850s period could be located**—no college calendars listing required texts, no course syllabi from the period, and no direct evidence that pro-Currency School texts "dominated" or that Banking School texts (Tooke, Fullarton) were systematically "excluded."

**Citation:** "Principles of Political Economy," citing Eric Roll, *History of Economic Thought* and *Oxford Dictionary of National Biography*.

#### Critical Evidentiary Gaps

**Examination Questions:** Despite extensive searching, **no surviving examination papers in political economy from Oxford or Cambridge for the 1820s-1850s period could be located**. One source documents examination papers from Cambridge for the 1871-1872 academic year, but this falls two decades after the target period and provides limited evidence for assessing 1840s-1850s orthodoxy.

**Citation:** Irwin Collier, "Cambridge. Examination Papers in Political Economy, 1871-1872," *Economics in the Rear-View Mirror* blog.

**Senior's Currency School Alignment:** While Senior published *Three Lectures on the Cost of obtaining Money* (1830) during his first Drummond professorship and actively participated in monetary policy debates, **no direct evidence establishes that Senior explicitly taught Currency School doctrines in his Oxford lectures**. His first term (1825-1830) predates the full crystallization of the Currency-Banking debate, which intensified in the 1840s leading to the 1844 Act. The debate was most acute during Senior's second term (1847-1852), but no specific evidence documents his 1847-52 lecture content regarding these controversies.

**Heterodox Exclusion:** **No documented cases of heterodox economists denied positions at Oxford or Cambridge in the 1820s-1850s could be found**. No evidence emerged of controversies over appointments based on Currency School versus Banking School alignment, nor of deliberate exclusion of Banking School texts from curricula. The period's major academic gatekeeping centered on religious tests (subscription to the Thirty-Nine Articles) for admitting Nonconformists and Catholics, not economic doctrine.

**Enrollment Data:** Very limited evidence exists. Pryme's lectures were initially "well attended" while Senior's suffered "disappointingly decreasing audience." For the Moral Sciences Tripos (which included political economy), "during the first nine years only 66 competed for honours in moral [sciences]" (1848-1857). Political economy was not required but optional within the broader tripos. No specific enrollment figures for Oxford's Drummond Professor lectures in the 1820s-1850s could be located.

#### Assessment for Curriculum Capture Thesis

**Evidence supporting institutional presence:**
- Currency School achieved major legislative victory (Bank Charter Act 1844)
- Nassau Senior held prestigious Drummond Professorship twice (1825-30, 1847-52)
- Senior published extensively on monetary topics during Oxford tenure
- Mill's *Principles* (1848), which engaged with Currency-Banking debates, became dominant textbook

**Critical weaknesses in evidence base:**
- No "smoking gun" documents showing deliberate curriculum control
- No examination papers to prove students were tested on Currency School orthodoxy
- No textbook assignment lists showing Banking School exclusion
- Banking School figures also held positions: Thomas Tooke was prominent without evidence of academic marginalization
- Chronology problem: Full Currency-Banking debate intensified in 1840s; Senior's influential first term was 1825-30
- Religious tests were the real academic gatekeepers in 1820s-1850s Oxbridge, not economic doctrine

**While Currency School ideas certainly circulated in Oxbridge teaching during the 1820s-1850s, primary source evidence for deliberate "institutional control" or "manufactured orthodoxy" through curriculum capture remains elusive.** A definitive study would require archival research at Oxford and Cambridge university libraries, examination of Drummond Professor papers, college teaching records, student notebooks and examination scripts (if extant), and private correspondence regarding curriculum decisions.

## TIER 2 FINDINGS

### 3. Bank Charter Act 1844 Parliamentary Debates

#### Overview and Timeline

The Bank Charter Act 1844 (7 & 8 Vict. c. 32), receiving Royal Assent on July 19, 1844, and coming into force in September 1844, represented the complete institutional triumph of Currency School principles in British monetary policy. The Act divided the Bank of England into separate Issue and Banking Departments, imposed an automatic rule for note issue (£14 million fiduciary issue, with the remainder backed 100% by gold), restricted country bank note issue to 1844 levels, and prohibited new banks of issue.

**Major Parliamentary Debate Dates:**
- **May 6, 1844**: Peel's introduction of the bill (Commons)
- **May 20, 1844**: Peel's second speech (Commons)
- **June 13, 1844**: Second Reading with major opposition speeches (Commons)
- **June 24, 1844**: Committee stage (Commons)
- **June 27, 1844**: Further committee debate on Scotland and Ireland (Commons)
- **July 12, 1844**: Lords consideration (less contentious)
- **July 19, 1844**: Royal Assent

**Citation:** Robert Peel, *Parliamentary Debates, Commons*, 3rd ser., vol. 74 (6 May 1844), cols. 720-754; *Parliamentary Debates, Commons*, 3rd ser., vol. 75 (13 June 1844), cols. 777-833.

#### Peel's Currency School Arguments

Prime Minister Sir Robert Peel's May 6, 1844 speech articulated the Currency School's fundamental principles with exceptional clarity:

**On the Definition of the Pound:**

"My first question, therefore, is, what constitutes this Measure of Value? What is the signification of that word 'a Pound,' with which we are all familiar? What is the engagement to pay a 'Pound'?... the whole foundation of the proposal I am about to make rests upon the assumption that according to practice, according to law, according to the ancient monetary policy of this country, that which is implied by the word 'Pound' is a certain definite quantity of gold with a mark upon it to determine its weight and fineness, and that the engagement to pay a Pound means nothing, and can mean nothing else, than the promise to pay to the holder, when he demands it that definite quantity of gold."

**Citation:** Robert Peel, *Parliamentary Debates, Commons*, 3rd ser., vol. 74 (6 May 1844), col. 723.

**On Limiting Competition in Note Issue:**

"We think it of great importance to increase the controlling power of a single Bank of issue... If we admit the principle of a metallic standard, and admit that the paper currency ought to be regulated by immediate reference to the foreign exchanges,—that there ought to be early contractions of paper on the efflux of gold,—we might I think infer from reasoning, without the aid of experience, that an unlimited competition in respect to issue will not afford a security for the proper regulation of the paper currency."

**Citation:** Robert Peel, *Parliamentary Debates, Commons*, 3rd ser., vol. 74 (6 May 1844), cols. 779-780.

**On the Two Department System:**

"It is proposed that the Bank of England shall continue in possession of its present privileges... Two Departments of the Bank will be constituted: one for the issue of notes, the other for the transaction of the ordinary business of banking. The bullion now in the possession of the Bank will be transferred to the Issue Department. The issue of notes will be restricted to an issue of £14,000,000 upon securities - the remainder being issued upon bullion - and governed in amount by the fluctuations in the stock of bullion."

**Citation:** Robert Peel, *Parliamentary Debates, Commons*, 3rd ser., vol. 74 (6 May 1844), col. 750.

**On the Act as Completion of 1819 Resumption:**

"Considering the part which I took in the year 1819 in terminating the system of inconvertible paper currency, and in re-establishing the ancient standard of value, it will no doubt be a source of great personal satisfaction to me, if I shall now succeed, after the lapse of a quarter of a century since those measures were adopted, in obtaining the assent of the House to proposals which are, in fact, the complement of them."

**Citation:** Robert Peel, *Parliamentary Debates, Commons*, 3rd ser., vol. 74 (6 May 1844), col. 754.

Peel explicitly framed the 1844 Act as fulfilling the promise of the 1819 Resumption Act, which he had championed as a young MP, thereby establishing a twenty-five-year arc of Currency School institutional influence.

#### Banking School Opposition in Parliament

**Mr. Benjamin Hawes (Lambeth)** led parliamentary opposition, moving on June 13, 1844: "That no sufficient evidence has been laid before this House to justify the proposed interference with banks of issue in the management of their circulation."

**On Convertibility as Sufficient Safeguard:**

"I subscribe to what is contained in that portion of his speech [Peel's] as completely and as cordially as the right hon. Baronet himself... But I do mean to contend that that principle, or those doctrines, afford no sufficient basis for the proposed interference with the Banks of issue in the management of their circulation. This measure is intended, avowedly, to surround the principle of the convertibility with additional security beyond that offered by the Bill of 1819... If promissory notes payable on demand are convertible into gold at the will of the holder, I contend that so long as such convertibility is maintained and secured by law and practice, there is an adequate and sufficient check upon the issues of bank notes."

**Citation:** Benjamin Hawes, *Parliamentary Debates, Commons*, 3rd ser., vol. 75 (13 June 1844), cols. 778-780.

**On Lack of Evidence for Over-Issue:**

"I have nowhere met with any proof of that assertion; I have never seen, although I have diligently searched for something of the kind, any attempt to bring together in the relation of cause and effect, the over issue of convertible paper, and a high price of commodities. Neither the evidence before the Bullion Committee, nor that taken before the Committee on banks of issue, contain any statement of facts in proof of this assertion."

**Citation:** Benjamin Hawes, *Parliamentary Debates, Commons*, 3rd ser., vol. 75 (13 June 1844), col. 786.

**Prophetic Warning:**

"I believe, you can no more prevent speculation than you can prevent smuggling by legislative enactments, if the temptation be too great; and I think this Measure will tend to enhance the temptation... if you attempt, by legislation, to reduce the legal currency below the natural requirements of this country, the necessity for a circulating medium of some kind will prove too strong for your legislation, and will lead to a system of dealing in small bills and acceptances objectionable enough."

**Citation:** Benjamin Hawes, *Parliamentary Debates, Commons*, 3rd ser., vol. 75 (13 June 1844), col. 831.

This prediction proved accurate: the Act was suspended during crises in 1847, 1857, and 1866, and credit did indeed find alternative forms through bills of exchange.

**Mr. Hastie** seconded Hawes's amendment with stark warning: "If this plan were to come suddenly into operation, after a period of increased prices, the ruin which it would bring upon the whole country would be a thousand times greater than in 1825 or 1839... There would be such an utter collapse in so short a period, that it must be plain to every man of experience that no bank in London could stand it; no merchant in London could stand it."

**Citation:** Mr. Hastie, *Parliamentary Debates, Commons*, 3rd ser., vol. 75 (13 June 1844), cols. 806-807.

**Mr. Charles Newdegate** articulated the country interest's concern about currency contraction's effects on agricultural prices: "A reduction of prices caused by a contraction of the Currency must ever be the most detrimental to our industrious classes, for to them it is a sheer loss without the shadow of an equivalent."

**Citation:** Charles Newdegate, *Parliamentary Debates, Commons*, 3rd ser., vol. 75 (13 June 1844), cols. 829-830.

#### The Bank Restriction Period (1797-1821) in Debate

The Bank Restriction period—when the Bank of England suspended gold convertibility—featured in debates but not as a positive example for paper money. Peel characterized it as a period of dangerous delusion:

"From 1797 to 1810 public attention was not much directed to this important subject; but in 1810 men of sagacity observed that the exchanges had been for a considerable period unfavourable to this country... The minds of men, habituated during the Bank Restriction to a departure from that measure of value, were loath to admit those great elementary truths which are at the foundation of the whole system of currency, paper credit, and foreign exchange."

**Citation:** Robert Peel, *Parliamentary Debates, Commons*, 3rd ser., vol. 74 (6 May 1844), cols. 726-727.

Benjamin Hawes, however, cited Deacon Hume's contrarian assessment: "Our money at that time, (the period in question), was wholly paper, unchecked by gold as its test or regulator... Now, I mean to assert, that from this trial of its value our currency of that day will come out triumphant. It is a positive fact that England was the cheapest country in the world during the time when gold was 25 per cent. and upwards, above the mint price."

**Citation:** Benjamin Hawes citing Deacon Hume, *Parliamentary Debates, Commons*, 3rd ser., vol. 75 (13 June 1844), cols. 782-783.

**No references to Adam Smith's 1776 views on tax-driven money demand could be located in the parliamentary debates examined.**

#### Lord Overstone's Influence

**Samuel Jones Loyd** (1796-1883), not yet ennobled (he became Baron Overstone in 1860), served as the principal architect of Currency School theory and the Act's intellectual foundation. Though not an MP and thus absent from parliamentary speeches, his influence pervaded the debates through his extensive testimony before the 1840 Select Committee on Banks of Issue and his published works:

- *Reflections Suggested by a Perusal of Mr. J. Horsley Palmer's Pamphlet* (1837)
- *Further Reflections on the State of the Currency* (1837)
- *Remarks on the Management of the Circulation* (1840)
- *Thoughts on the Separation of the Departments of the Bank of England* (1844)

An 1857 parliamentary debate explicitly acknowledged his authorship: "The noble Lord opposite (Lord Overstone) one of its chief promoters, and generally supposed to be its author, upon the question of the note circulation keeping pace with the increase or decrease of gold, said— 'The issues of the Bank of England will, under the provisions of that Bill, be made to conform strictly to the fluctuations of the bullion.'"

**Citation:** *Parliamentary Debates, Lords*, 3rd ser., vol. 141 (7 April 1856), debate on "The Currency—The Bank Charter Act."

#### Vote Counts and Party Dynamics

**Critical Gap:** Despite extensive searching of digitized Hansard records, **no recorded division lists for the Bank Charter Act 1844's key votes could be located**. This comports with mid-19th-century parliamentary practice where many bills passed without formal divisions being called, and division lists were not always published in Hansard.

The debates make clear the Act enjoyed **overwhelming support**, with opposition speakers acknowledging their minority status. The controversy was **not primarily partisan** but rather intellectual—Currency School versus Banking School monetary theories—with both Conservatives and Liberals on each side. Sir William Clay, a Liberal, strongly supported Peel's Conservative government measure. Chancellor of the Exchequer Henry Goulburn noted that the measure "had consequently met with the concurrence of those who had attended to the Committee [1840-41], who had heard all the objections that could be urged against it, and had heard all the advantages that might be expected from it."

**Citation:** Henry Goulburn, *Parliamentary Debates, Commons*, 3rd ser., vol. 75 (13 June 1844), col. 809.

#### Related Parliamentary Inquiries

The **1840 Select Committee on Banks of Issue** provided the evidentiary foundation for the 1844 Act, hearing testimony from Samuel Jones Loyd and George Warde Norman (Currency School) and Thomas Tooke (Banking School), along with Bank of England Governor J. Horsley Palmer. This inquiry was extensively cited during the 1844 debates.

**Citation:** Great Britain, Parliament, House of Commons, *Report from the Select Committee on Banks of Issue*, Parliamentary Papers 1840 (602) IV.

Following the Act's suspension during the 1847 crisis, two committees examined its performance with divergent conclusions. The **1848 Commons Secret Committee on Commercial Distress** supported modifications by bare majority, while the **1848 Lords Secret Committee** defended the Act.

**Citation:** Great Britain, Parliament, House of Commons, *Report from the Secret Committee on Commercial Distress*, Parliamentary Papers 1847-48 (395) VIII; Great Britain, Parliament, House of Lords, *Report from the Secret Committee on Commercial Distress*, Parliamentary Papers 1847-48 (565) VIII.

## GAPS AND LIMITATIONS IN DOCUMENTATION

### Critical Evidentiary Gaps Requiring Archival Research

**1. Early Economist Articles (1843-1850):** No specific article quotes with volume and page numbers could be obtained through freely accessible digital sources. **Required:** Institutional access to Gale's Economist Historical Archive, British Library Newspapers collection, or physical consultation of Wilson's *Capital, Currency, and Banking* (1847) and Edwards' *The Pursuit of Reason* (1993).

**2. Examination Papers (1820s-1850s):** No surviving political economy examination papers from Oxford or Cambridge during the target period could be located. This represents the most significant gap for assessing whether students were tested on Currency School orthodoxy. **Required:** Archive visits to Oxford and Cambridge university libraries, examination of college records.

**3. Textbook Assignment Records:** No university calendars, course syllabi, or official records specifying which political economy texts were required reading in which years. **Required:** College and university archive research for teaching records, course lists, private correspondence of professors regarding curriculum.

**4. Nassau Senior's Currency School Alignment in Teaching:** While Senior published on monetary topics, no direct evidence establishes whether he explicitly taught Currency School doctrines in his Oxford lectures, particularly during his 1847-52 term contemporaneous with the Bank Charter Act. **Required:** Examination of Drummond Professor papers, student notebooks, college archives.

**5. Vote Tallies for Bank Charter Act 1844:** No recorded division lists specifying vote counts and individual MP positions could be located in digitized Hansard. **Required:** UK National Archives research for division lists if they exist; consultation of complete bound Hansard volumes.

**6. Evidence of Heterodox Exclusion:** No documented cases emerged of economists denied academic positions for heterodox views on monetary questions, nor evidence of deliberate exclusion of Banking School texts from curricula. **Required:** University appointment records, private correspondence of hiring authorities, college meeting minutes.

### What Remains Uncertain

**Causality of Institutional Capture:** While the Currency School achieved legislative victory (1844 Act) and prominent economists like Nassau Senior held prestigious academic positions, the research uncovered no "smoking gun" documents demonstrating **deliberate coordination** to manufacture orthodoxy through institutional control. The evidence is consistent with Currency School institutional presence but does not conclusively establish **orchestrated capture** as opposed to **natural influence** of successful ideas.

**The Economist's Role:** The finding that Wilson and The Economist were Banking School advocates fundamentally contradicts the chapter's premise but raises new questions: Did Currency School doctrine become orthodox **despite** Banking School control of an influential periodical? What other periodicals or publications supported Currency School views?

**Academic Teaching Content:** The evidentiary gaps regarding examination questions, textbook assignments, and lecture content make it impossible to conclusively demonstrate that Oxford and Cambridge **taught Currency School orthodoxy to the exclusion of Banking School views** during the 1820s-1850s. Religious tests, not economic doctrine, served as the primary academic gatekeepers during this period.

### Revised Thesis Framework

The research findings suggest reframing Chapter 9's argument from "The Economist as Currency School propaganda organ" to "Currency School captured Parliament while losing the press." This revised framework highlights:

1. **Legislative Capture (Successful):** Bank Charter Act 1844 represented complete Currency School triumph through Peel's government and overwhelming parliamentary support, based on Loyd's intellectual architecture and 1840 Committee evidence.

2. **Press Opposition (Failed):** James Wilson's Economist actively opposed the Currency School from 1843 onward, compiling Banking School critiques and publishing Wilson's, Tooke's, and Fullarton's counterarguments—yet lost the policy battle.

3. **Academic Presence (Ambiguous):** Nassau Senior held Oxford's prestigious Drummond Chair twice (1825-30, 1847-52) and published on monetary topics, but direct evidence of teaching Currency School orthodoxy remains elusive. Mill's *Principles* (1848) dominated as textbook, but Mill engaged both schools of thought.

4. **Institutional Victory Despite Intellectual Opposition:** The Currency School's success came not from controlling all channels of discourse but from capturing the decisive channel—Parliamentary legislation—while Banking School voices (Wilson, Tooke, Fullarton) remained audible but ultimately ineffective.

## CONCLUSION

This research has uncovered rigorous primary source documentation for Currency School institutional influence in **parliamentary legislation** (Bank Charter Act 1844) while simultaneously revealing that the chapter's premise regarding **The Economist as Currency School propaganda organ is factually incorrect**—Wilson and The Economist were Banking School advocates. Academic curriculum evidence shows Currency School figures held prestigious positions (Senior at Oxford) but lacks the "smoking gun" examination papers and textbook lists needed to conclusively demonstrate orchestrated curriculum capture.

The findings suggest Currency School institutional victory came through capturing **Parliament**—the decisive arena—while losing **the press** (Economist) and achieving ambiguous presence in **academia** (Senior's positions documented; teaching content uncertain). This pattern reveals a more complex story of institutional influence than simple across-the-board capture, one where strategic success in the key legislative arena outweighed losses in other domains of discourse.
