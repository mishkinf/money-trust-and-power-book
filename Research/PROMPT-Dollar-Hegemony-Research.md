## **Part 3: Historical Parallels to Gold Standard**

This connects dollar hegemony to the book's earlier chapters on the gold standard's deflationary bias and distributional consequences.

### **Research Questions:**

**3.1 Gold Standard Analogy**

Under gold standard (1870s-1930s):
- Core countries (Britain, later US) benefited from setting rules
- Peripheral countries (Argentina, Brazil, India) suffered deflation to maintain peg
- Led to Great Depression when system couldn't adjust

Under dollar standard (1971-present):
- Core country (US) benefits from "exorbitant privilege"
- Peripheral countries (Argentina, Turkey, etc.) suffer currency crises to maintain dollar access
- Creates recurring crises (1980s debt crisis, 1997 Asia crisis, 2018 emerging markets)

**Need specific evidence:**
- Did countries that stayed on gold standard longer suffer worse Depression outcomes? (Barry Eichengreen's research - find citations)
- Do countries with higher dollar-denominated debt suffer worse outcomes during Fed tightening cycles? (Quantitative studies)

**3.2 Distributional Consequences (Then and Now)**

**Gold standard distributional effects:**
- Creditors gained (deflation increased real value of debts)
- Debtors/workers lost (real wages fell, unemployment rose)
- Core countries gained, periphery suffered

**Dollar standard distributional effects:**
- Dollar asset holders gain (US investors, wealthy in emerging markets who can access dollars)
- Local currency earners lose (workers paid in pesos while prices rise from devaluation)
- Core (US) gains, periphery suffers

**Need data:**
- Capital flight from emerging markets during crises (how much wealth left Argentina in 2001, 2018?)
- Who holds dollar-denominated Argentine bonds? (Foreign investors vs. domestic elites)
- Inequality trends in Argentina vs. Japan over same time period

## **Part 6: Connection to Book's Narrative**

This research must connect to the book's existing themes. Provide evidence for these arguments:

**6.1 "Institutions Matter More Than Metal" (or Dollars)**

The book argues gold standard failed because it was institutional choice with distributional consequences, not natural monetary system. Same for dollar standard:
- Dollar hegemony is institutional arrangement (Bretton Woods), not inevitable
- Creates distributional consequences (core benefits, periphery suffers)
- Can be changed through institutional reform (just as gold standard was abandoned)

**6.2 "Taxation Creates Currency Demand" (Expanded Globally)**

- For Argentina: must pay dollar-denominated debts creates dollar demand, just as taxation creates peso demand domestically
- For global system: need for dollars to buy oil/commodities creates dollar demand worldwide
- This is STRUCTURAL demand, not market demand - equivalent to taxation

**6.3 "Money is Power"**

- Dollar dominance is power relationship: US sets Fed policy for domestic needs, affects whole world
- Distributional consequences: wealth flows from periphery to core through debt service
- Political implications: developing countries structurally subordinated

**6.4 "Real Resources Matter, Not Nominal Money"**

- Argentina's crises happen when real resources (commodity exports) fluctuate, not because of peso printing per se
- Japan avoids crises despite massive deficits because debt in own currency allows real resource mobilization
- Confirms book's thesis: real constraint is productive capacity, not nominal money supply
