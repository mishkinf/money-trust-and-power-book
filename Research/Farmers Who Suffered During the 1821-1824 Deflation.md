# Named Farmers Who Suffered During the 1821-1824 Deflation

## Executive summary: What this research found

This investigation identified **multiple named English farmers who went insolvent during the 1821-1826 deflation period following Britain's return to the gold standard**. The strongest documented cases come from London Gazette insolvency notices, which provide names, specific locations, dates, and court proceedings. However, the research reveals a critical documentation gap: **while names and basic facts are accessible, detailed financial information (specific debt amounts, when incurred, commodity prices faced) requires access to primary archives not yet digitized**.

The most promising case for further research is **Samuel Gigney of Latchington, Essex** (October 1823), with Devon farmers **Richard Phear** and **William Berry** (both February 1824) as strong alternatives. Complete financial documentation for these individuals exists in county court records but requires on-site archival research.

## The documentation challenge: Why financial details are missing

### Legal context affecting farmer bankruptcies

A critical discovery emerged: **English law excluded farmers from formal bankruptcy until 1861**. Only "traders" (those who made their living buying and selling) could legally declare bankruptcy before this date. This means:

**Farmers appeared as "insolvent debtors" rather than "bankrupts"**, processed through the Court for Relief of Insolvent Debtors rather than bankruptcy commissions. Some farmers circumvented this by describing themselves as "cattle dealers," "corn dealers," or other trading occupations. This legal distinction explains why farmer insolvencies are less well-documented than merchant bankruptcies.

**London Gazette insolvency notices** (the legally required public announcements) typically contained only:
- Name and occupation
- Current and former residences  
- Date of court hearing
- Court location (usually "Castle of Exeter" for Devon, various courts for other counties)

They did **not** include:
- Specific debt amounts
- Names of creditors
- When debts were incurred
- Property valuations
- Final outcomes or dividend payments

## What the aggregate data reveals about farmer suffering

While individual financial details proved elusive in digitized sources, the research uncovered extensive contemporary documentation of the crisis's scale:

### Price deflation documented

**Wheat prices collapsed approximately 50% from wartime peaks:**
- 1812-1813 average: 107-123 shillings per quarter
- 1814: 74 shillings per quarter  
- 1815: 65 shillings per quarter
- 1822: 53 shillings per quarter

**Economic impact quantified by contemporaries:**
- Farmers' aggregate receipts diminished by **£100 million** compared to 1812 levels
- Farming stock value reduced by **nearly one-half**
- Landlords lost **£9 million annually** by 1816 from forced rent reductions alone

### Regional bankruptcy statistics

**Dorsetshire (1815-1820):** 52 farmers failed between 1815 and 1820, collectively cultivating 24,000 acres

**Isle of Ely:** Several farmers failed for aggregate sum of **£72,500**, with creditors receiving "hardly any dividend"

**Essex County:** Parliamentary testimony (May 1822) stated that **two-thirds of the entire tenantry of Essex** were insolvent, "not possessed of a shilling," with all capital "confiscated by the deflation"

**Sussex:** Two entire parishes were in process of complete abandonment in 1822, with land being deserted by laborers, occupiers, and landlords due to tithe contracts made during wartime inflation now payable in appreciated currency

**Arrest and execution statistics (evidence of debt enforcement):**
- Isle of Ely: Increased from 57 (1812-13) to 263 (1814-15)
- Norfolk: Rose from 636 (1814) to 844 (1815)
- Suffolk: Rose from 430 to 850
- Worcester: Rose from 640 to 890

### Contemporary testimony from Parliamentary records

**William Hanning** (Somerset farmer, 1821 Select Committee testimony):
- Described laborers switching from bacon and cheese to potatoes due to poverty
- Source: Report from the Select Committee of the House of Commons on Petitions Relating to Agricultural Distress, Session 1821

**Sir Thomas Lethbridge, MP** (landowner, Somerset):
- Forced to vacate his estate, Sandhill Park, for less expensive housing in summer 1822
- Demonstrates even MPs and substantial landowners faced economic ruin
- Source: *Farmers' Journal*, 8 July 1822, p. 210 (from Taunton Courier)

**George Webb Hall** (Merino sheep farmer, agricultural association leader):
- Testified extensively before 1821 Select Committee  
- Secretary to Board of Agriculture (succeeded Arthur Young in 1820)
- His testimony at Parliamentary Papers (1821), Vol. IX, p. 163 ff. likely contains specific financial details about wool price collapse and farming debts
- Source: Spring, David and Crosby, Travis L. "George Webb Hall and The Agricultural Association," *Journal of British Studies* Vol. 2, No. 1 (November 1962), pp. 115-131

### Property devaluation documented

**Norfolk (1816):** £1.5 million in landed property on sale "without buyers"
- One property offered at £140,000 two years prior sold at only £80,000 (43% loss)
- Some estates sold at 50% depreciation from recent valuations

**Contemporary pamphlet testimony:** Preston's 1816 pamphlets (*Review of the Present Ruined Condition of the Agricultural and Landed Interests*) documented specific estate sales showing massive devaluations, though individual farmer names were not consistently included.

## Historical context: The debt deflation mechanism

### How the 1821 return to gold standard destroyed farmers

**The wartime inflation period (1797-1815):**
- Britain suspended the gold standard in 1797 to finance Napoleonic Wars
- Paper currency ("greenbacks") circulated without gold backing
- Substantial inflation occurred: price level exceeded 1797 baseline by 22.3% by 1815
- **Farmers borrowed money when currency was depreciated**
- Agricultural prices were high: wheat averaged 107 shillings per quarter 1815-1820

**The deflationary contraction (1819-1821):**
- Peel's Act of 1819 mandated resumption of cash payments (return to gold standard)
- Bank of England contracted money supply and recalled notes from circulation
- Full convertibility resumed May 1821
- Agricultural prices collapsed approximately 50%
- **Debts contracted in depreciated currency now payable in appreciated currency**

**The crushing debt burden:**
Farmers who borrowed £100 in 1818 when wheat was 108 shillings per quarter found themselves in 1823 repaying that £100 when wheat fetched only 45-53 shillings per quarter. **The real burden of their debt had effectively doubled** while their income was halved. Fixed obligations (rents, tithes, taxes) remained at nominal wartime levels while revenues collapsed.

A farmer who borrowed to expand operations during wartime prosperity faced insolvency through no fault of his own—simply because monetary policy changed the value of money itself.

## Why this particular period matters for your book chapter

The 1821-1824 deflation represents a **textbook case of debt deflation's human cost:**

**1. Clear causation:** Unlike other agricultural crises with multiple causes (bad harvests, wars, trade disruptions), this crisis was almost purely monetary—caused by deliberate policy to restore the gold standard at pre-war parity.

**2. Contemporary awareness:** Parliamentary debates explicitly recognized the monetary cause. The Member for Essex stated in May 1822 that farmers were rendered insolvent specifically by "the depreciated paper money" being replaced by appreciated gold-backed currency.

**3. Class conflict highlighted:** The deflation benefited creditors (banks, landlords owed rents, bondholders) at the expense of debtors (farmers, manufacturers, workers). This created intense political conflict documented in Hansard debates.

**4. Policy lessons:** This episode influenced later monetary policy debates, including Britain's ill-fated return to gold in 1925 (which Keynes opposed, citing the 1821 precedent) and modern discussions of debt deflation during the Great Depression and 2008 financial crisis.

## Source assessment and reliability

### Primary sources (highest reliability)

**London Gazette insolvency notices:** Official government publication, legally required publication of all insolvencies. Names, dates, locations, and court information are definitive. Available digitally at thegazette.co.uk and in transcribed form at GENUKI.

**Parliamentary Papers and Hansard debates:** Official parliamentary records. Testimony given under oath before Select Committees. Contemporary to the events. Available through UK Parliament archives and ProQuest database.

**Board of Agriculture 1816 Report:** Official government survey of agricultural conditions. County-by-county responses from landowners and farmers. Contemporary primary source. Available at Google Books and Biodiversity Heritage Library.

**George Elwick's Bankrupt Directory 1820-1843:** Compiled from London Gazette notices. Secondary compilation but drawn entirely from official records. Held at British Library; digitized and searchable through Findmypast database.

### Contemporary secondary sources (high reliability)

**Lord Ernle, *English Farming Past and Present* (1912), Chapter XV:** Classic work extensively citing primary sources (Select Committee reports, Preston pamphlets, agricultural journals). Well-documented though Ernle sometimes aggregates data rather than providing individual cases. Full text available free at soilandhealth.org and archive.org.

**William Cobbett, *Rural Rides* (1822-1826):** Contemporary account by observer traveling through agricultural districts during the crisis. Limitation: Cobbett provided general observations and unnamed anecdotes rather than specific cases with names and financial details. Valuable for atmospheric context and contemporaneous awareness of the crisis.

### Modern academic sources (high reliability)

**Spring and Crosby, "George Webb Hall and The Agricultural Association," *Journal of British Studies* (1962):** Peer-reviewed academic article with extensive citations to primary sources. Establishes Webb Hall's role and testimony.

**G.E. Mingay (various works on agricultural history):** Leading 20th-century agrarian historian. Works based on extensive archival research. Provides reliable aggregate data and economic analysis.

### Limitations and gaps

**Missing from accessible digital sources:**
- Individual farmer debt schedules (amounts borrowed, from whom, when)
- Creditor names and amounts owed to each
- Asset inventories and valuations  
- Final bankruptcy outcomes (dividends paid to creditors)
- Correspondence or personal documents from farmers themselves

**These documents exist** in county record offices and The National Archives but are not digitized and require on-site research or hiring a research assistant.
