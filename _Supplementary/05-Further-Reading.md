# Further Reading: An Annotated Guide

*Organized by theme, with brief descriptions to guide exploration*

---

## **ON THE CREDIT THEORY OF MONEY**

### **David Graeber, *Debt: The First 5,000 Years* (2011)**
Anthropological tour de force arguing money emerged from social obligations, not barter. Graeber traces credit systems from Mesopotamian temples through medieval tallies to modern finance. Sometimes polemical but essential for understanding money-as-relationship rather than money-as-thing. Read for the historical scope and challenge to orthodox economics.

### **Michael Hudson, *...and forgive them their debts* (2018)**
Economic historian's detailed examination of ancient Near Eastern debt cancellations (*amargi*). Hudson demonstrates how Bronze Age societies prevented debt bondage through periodic jubilees, arguing this institutional innovation was central to their stability. Technical but rewards careful reading for its revolutionary implications about debt's political nature.

### **A. Mitchell Innes, "What is Money?" (1913)**
Foundational essay arguing money is credit, not commodity. Innes shows how medieval tally sticks and modern currency both function as transferable obligations, not stores of value. Surprisingly readable for century-old economic theory; directly influenced modern credit theorists. Available in *Banking Law Journal* archives.

### **L. Randall Wray, *Modern Money Theory* (2012)**
Comprehensive introduction to MMT's credit view of money. Wray traces how taxation creates demand for fiat currency and explains government finance through lens of state money. Useful synthesis of chartalist tradition from Knapp through Innes to present. Best overview of taxation-driven currency demand.

## **ON THE GOLD STANDARD**

### **Barry Eichengreen, *Golden Fetters: The Gold Standard and the Great Depression* (1992)**
Definitive account of how gold standard rigidity deepened 1930s Depression. Eichengreen shows countries that abandoned gold earlier recovered faster, demonstrating the standard's deflationary bias. Essential for understanding the system's vulnerabilities and why it ultimately failed.

### **Michael D. Bordo & Hugh Rockoff, "The Gold Standard as a 'Good Housekeeping Seal of Approval'" (1996)**
Economic analysis of gold standard as credibility device. Authors argue adherence signaled fiscal discipline to international lenders, lowering borrowing costs. Explains why peripheral countries adopted gold despite domestic costs. Key for understanding the standard as institutional technology rather than neutral monetary rule.

### **Marc Flandreau, "The French Crime of 1873: An Essay on the Emergence of the International Gold Standard" (1996)**
Revisionist history showing gold standard's rise was contingent, not inevitable. Flandreau traces how network effects and French policy shifts created coordination around gold. Challenges teleological narratives; reveals monetary regimes' political foundations.

---

## **ON THE FINANCIAL REVOLUTION & BRITISH INSTITUTIONS**

### **Douglass North & Barry Weingast, "Constitutions and Commitment" (1989)**
Landmark political economy analysis arguing Glorious Revolution's institutional changes made English government credible borrower. Authors show how parliamentary control over taxation lowered interest rates and enabled Britain's rise. Foundation for understanding institutional credibility's role in public finance.

### **John Brewer, *The Sinews of Power: War, Money and the English State* (1989)**
Comprehensive history of Britain's 18th-century fiscal-military state. Brewer details how parliamentary taxation, excise administration, and Bank of England combined to finance wars that made Britain global power. Readable yet rigorous; essential for understanding link between institutions and imperial expansion.

### **P.G.M. Dickson, *The Financial Revolution in England* (1967)**
Detailed scholarly study of 1688–1756 transformation of English public finance. Dickson traces emergence of funded debt, growth of Bank of England, and development of securities markets. Dense but authoritative; primary source for understanding mechanics of early modern state finance.

### **Patrick O'Brien & Nuno Palma, "Danger to the Old Lady of Threadneedle Street?" (2020)**
Modern reassessment showing Bank of England's early fragility. Challenges triumphalist narratives by revealing how close the Bank came to failure in 1690s–1720s. Important corrective emphasizing contingency and institutional learning over inevitability.

---

## **ON CENTRAL BANKING & MONETARY POLICY**

### **John Clapham, *The Bank of England: A History* (1944)**
Two-volume official history covering 1694–1914. Clapham had unprecedented access to Bank archives; result is authoritative, detailed chronicle. Dense and institutional-focused, but indispensable for understanding Bank's evolution from government contractor to central bank proper.

### **Claudio Borio, "On Money, Debt, Trust and Central Banking" (2019)**
BIS economist's synthetic essay on central banking's evolution. Borio argues institutional credibility matters more than gold or rules; examines how central banks became "key pillars" of monetary trust. Accessible synthesis of historical lessons for contemporary policy.

### **Charles Goodhart, *The Evolution of Central Banks* (1988)**
Comparative history examining how central banks emerged in different national contexts. Goodhart identifies common patterns (lender of last resort function, note monopoly) while respecting institutional diversity. Clear framework for understanding central banking as institutional form.

---

## **ON BRETTON WOODS & THE FIAT ERA**

### **Benn Steil, *The Battle of Bretton Woods* (2013)**
Lively narrative of 1944 conference focusing on clash between Keynes's clearing union proposal and White's dollar-based plan. Steil makes complex negotiations accessible while showing how American power shaped postwar monetary order. Best single-volume introduction to Bretton Woods creation.

### **Barry Eichengreen, *Exorbitant Privilege: The Rise and Fall of the Dollar* (2011)**
Analysis of dollar's role as reserve currency from Bretton Woods through 21st century. Eichengreen examines both benefits (seigniorage, low borrowing costs) and costs (external constraints on policy) of reserve currency status. Essential for understanding post-1971 fiat era's political economy.

### **Michael Bordo & Harold James, "The European Crisis in the Context of the History of Previous Financial Crises" (2013)**
Comparative analysis placing euro crisis in long historical context. Authors show recurring patterns across regime breakdowns, from gold standard suspensions to Bretton Woods collapse. Demonstrates how institutional rigidity repeatedly creates crisis conditions.

## **ON WAR & MONETARY HISTORY**

### **Niall Ferguson, *The Cash Nexus: Money and Power in the Modern World* (2001)**
Sweeping survey of public finance's relationship to state power across five centuries. Ferguson argues fiscal capacity determined which states survived; traces how war drove financial innovation. Readable synthesis connecting monetary history to geopolitical outcomes.

### **Eugene N. White, "Making the French Pay: The Costs and Consequences of the Napoleonic Reparations" (2001)**
Case study of early 19th-century war finance and its aftermath. White shows how indemnity payments shaped French financial development and international credit markets. Illuminates links between war settlement, fiscal capacity, and monetary regime choice.

### **Hew Strachan, *Financing the First World War* (2004)**
Detailed examination of WWI's fiscal impact on belligerents. Strachan traces how war costs broke gold standard and transformed state-finance relationships. Essential for understanding the Great War as monetary turning point, not just military conflict.

---

## **THEORETICAL & METHODOLOGICAL FOUNDATIONS**

### **Moses I. Finley, *The Ancient Economy* (1973)**
Influential classical historian's analysis of pre-modern economic systems. Finley argues against anachronistic application of modern market concepts to ancient societies. Essential methodological warning against projecting capitalist categories backward; foundational for understanding temple economies.

### **Karl Polanyi, *The Great Transformation* (1944)**
Classic study arguing market economy is historically exceptional, embedded in social and political structures. Polanyi's concept of "embedded economy" essential for understanding how pre-modern money was inseparable from social obligation. Read for conceptual framework, though specific claims contested.

### **Geoffrey Ingham, *The Nature of Money* (2004)**
Sociological synthesis challenging orthodox economic theories of money's origins. Ingham draws on Weber, Simmel, and Keynes to argue money is social relation constituted by authority. Theoretical but accessible; useful for conceptual clarity on money-as-institution.

## **PRIMARY SOURCES & ARCHIVES (ONLINE)**

### **Bank of England Archive**
[www.bankofengland.co.uk/archive](http://www.bankofengland.co.uk/archive)  
Digitized Court of Directors minutes, banknote designs, and historical records. Invaluable for tracing Bank's evolution and policy debates.

### **UK Parliamentary Archives: Financial Revolution**
[www.parliament.uk/about/living-heritage/evolutionofparliament/parliamentaryauthority/revolution](http://www.parliament.uk/about/living-heritage/evolutionofparliament/parliamentaryauthority/revolution)  
Parliamentary records documenting 1689–1714 fiscal transformation. Primary sources on taxation debates, Bank charter, and budget reforms.

### **Federal Reserve History**
[www.federalreservehistory.org](http://www.federalreservehistory.org)  
Comprehensive timeline with essays on key episodes (Bretton Woods, Nixon Shock, 2008 crisis). Well-sourced introductions to 20th-century monetary events.

### **NBER Working Papers on Monetary History**
[www.nber.org/papers](http://www.nber.org/papers)  
Academic research on specific episodes and comparative analyses. Search for Bordo, Eichengreen, Flandreau for historical monetary economics.

## **HOW TO USE THIS GUIDE**

**For General Readers:** Start with Graeber (*Debt*), Steil (*Bretton Woods*), and Ferguson (*Cash Nexus*) for accessible narratives. Add Eichengreen's *Golden Fetters* to understand gold standard's failure.

**For Deep Dives:** Follow themes—if credit theory interests you, read Innes → Hudson → Wray. If institutions matter, read North & Weingast → Brewer → Dickson. Each section builds from accessible syntheses to detailed studies.

**For Academic Study:** Primary sources in Bank of England and Parliamentary archives provide raw material. Pair with theoretical frameworks from Ingham and methodological cautions from Finley.

**For Contemporary Policy:** Borio, Eichengreen's *Exorbitant Privilege*, and NBER papers connect historical lessons to current debates. Understanding past regime transitions illuminates present challenges.
